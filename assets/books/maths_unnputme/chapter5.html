Variation

“Variation” defines a concept that deals with variability in mathematics. Variation is defined by any change in some quantity due to change in another. We often come across with different types of variation problems in mathematics. Variation problems involve fairly simple relationships or formulas, involving one variable being equal to one term. Here follows the most common kinds of variation.

Direct or Proportional Variation: An increase in a variable lead to an increase in another variable.

 Example, in economics an increase In demand will lead to an increase in price

 We can say d α P (where d = demand  and p =price and  α = variation sign ).

 

Direct variation is always in the form


Y = kxn

 

Note in reality our constant K stands for other factors that we are not considering despite the facts that they could contribute to the formula like in the above case, time of the year can affect price, cost of production can as well affect price but we keep them constant, but theoritically that constant stand for slope or rate meaning how change in  Demand(d) cause anothet change in price (p).

 

Example

If y varies directly as x2 and y = 8 when x = 2, find y when x = 1.

 

Solution

Since this is direct variation, the formula is "y = kx2".The reason they've given me the data point (x, y) = (2, 8) is that I have to be able to find the value of "k". 

y = k x2

8 = k (2 x2)

8 = 4k

2 = k

Now that I have k, I can rewrite the formula completely:

y = 2x2

With this, I can answer the question they actually asked: "Find y when x = 1."

y = 2x2

y = 2 (1)2

y = 2 (1)

y = 2

Then the answer is:

y = 2

 

 

 

 

Inverse or Indirect Variation

An increase in a variable lead to an decrease in another variable.

 Example, in economics an increase In demand of snack will lead to a decrease in the demand of food

 We can say S α 1/F ( where S= snack and f=food and α= variation sign ).

 

Inverse Variation is always in the form


Y = k(1/xn)

 

Where K is a constant for rate or slope.

Joint and Combined Variation

One variable quantity is said to vary jointly as a number of other variable quantities, when it varies directly as their product. If the variable A varies directly as the product of the variables B, C and D, i.e., if.A ∝ BCD or A = kBCD (k = constant ), then A varies jointly as B, C and D.

 

We know, area of a triangle = ½ × base × altitude. Since ½ is a constant, hence area of a triangle varies oint1y as its base and altitude.

 

A is said to vary directly as B and inversely as C if A ∝ B ∙ 1/C or A = K ∙ B ∙ 1/C (k = constant of variation) i.e., if A varies jointly as B and 1/C.

Partial Variation

Partial variation exists when a quantity is partly constant and partly varies with

another quantity. For example, when a tailor makes a dress, the total cost

depends on two things; first the cost of the material (which is partly constant);

secondly the amount of time taken to make the dress (partly varies). This is an

example of partial variation i.e.

C = a + Kt

Where C is the cost and t is time taken, while “a” and “k” are constants.
