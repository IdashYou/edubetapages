18. FINANCIAL INSTITUTION

Financial institutions are establishments which deal on money or financial instruments. Examples of financial institutions are Central Bank, commercial Banks, Insurance Companies, Stock Exchange, Development Banks, Merchant Banks, stock exchange etc. 

What is a Bank? 

A Bank is a financial institution established to keep money, documents and other valuable goods safe. 

There are different types of banks which are Central, Commercial, Industrial, Merchant, Savings, Mortgage and development banks.

They accept deposits from the public and give them as loans to those who want to make use of them with interests. 

Banking financial institutions include central bank and commercial banks. 

Non-banking financial institutions are insurance companies, development bank, stock exchange, building societies, finance companies etc. 

Central bank is the lender of last resort. 

Financial market is divided into two: capital market and money market. 

Universal banking combines the activities of both banking and non-banking financial institutions. 

The Commercial Banks (Money Deposit Banks)

Commercial banks are financial institutions that perform the services of holding people’s money and accounts and using such money to make loans and other financial services available to customers, the loans are usually for short and medium terms. 

Commercial banks are public limited liability companies. Their aim is to maximize profits, unlike other banks they honour cheques drawn by their customers. on demand deposits (that is current account). They are known as Money Deposits Banks. 

Functions of Commercial Bank

  1. Money creating functions
  2. Acceptance of deposit
  3. Lending of money
  4. Money transfers
  5. Agency services
  6. Safekeeping of valuables
  7. Provide trust service
  8. Safety keeping of other valuables

 

Types of bank Account

1. Savings Account: the main purpose for savings account is to promote saving habit. It is the most common type of account to the low-income group and even undergraduates. There is no interest at the end of the month if the owner withdraws more than two times

2. Current Account: this account is mostly used by business men and other groups that make use of money frequently. They operate with cheques and cheques paid into them mature after four working days before they can be withdrawn. They enjoy other services like overdraft and bankdrafts.

3. Fixed Deposit Account (Time deposit Account): the money deposited in this type of account is for a specific purpose. Money is deposited in it for a specific period of time. A notice of withdrawal is giving to the bank 7-14 days before the money can be withdrawn. The Bank issues the owner of a fixed deposit account a receipt (deposit account passbook) and also pays interest to the owner depending on the amount deposited.

Commercial Banks Credit Facilities

1. Loan: this is the sum of money given to a customer or anyone that meets the requirement of the commercial bank. Collateral is always demanded from the individual. Collateral securities are fixed assets which the borrower must mortgage and the bank can auction them if the borrower fails to comply.

2. Overdraft: this is a credit facility enjoyed by those that operates with current account. It makes it possible for the customer to withdraw an amount more than what he has in account on interest.

Creation of Money (Credit) by Commercial Banks 

Commercial banks create credits by giving loans and overdraft to customers. When customers deposit money in the bank, the bank is required to keep the legal reserve (for example 10%), before tending out the rest to people who want to borrow them. 

Assumptions in the Ability of Banks to create Money

1. Banks invest only in Loans and Overdraft

2. There is nocrash drains from the banks

3. There is no excess reserve

4. There is only one type of deposit which is demand deposit

Limitations to the power of commercial Banks to create Credits

  1. The cash deposit ratio also known as legal reserve
  2. Collateral security available
  3. Central bank actions to restrict lending
  4. Amount of cash drain or leakage from the banking system
  5. Clearing house limitation
  6. Minimum lending rate
  7. Willingness of the public to take loans
  8. The desire of the public to save
  9. Total amount of cash in existence

 

The Central Bank (CBN) 

The Central Bank is a government owned bank which is responsible for issuing of currency and control of monetary policy in the country. It is established by an act of parliament. The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) was established in 1958 and started operating in 1959, after the dissolution of West African Currency Bank (WACB), which served the four British colonies of Nigeria, Ghana, Sierra Leone and Gambia. It has Board of Directors who is responsible for its management with the head as the Governor of the Central Bank. Central Bank is at the apex of the banking institutions. 

 

Functions of the Central Bank 

  1. Issuing of Currency
  2. Acts as Lender of last resort
  3. The banker and financial adviser to the government
  4. Acts as bankers' bank
  5. Regulation of the financial base for the banking industry
  6. Maintenance of monetary/price stability
  7. It develops money and capital markets
  8. Manages the country’s external reserves
  9. Encouragement of exports
  10. Servicing of national dept

 

Control of commercial banks by the Central bank 

The Central Bank implements its monetary policy through the commercial banks. Monetary policy controls the money supply in the economy. Therefore, for the Central Bank to achieve its monetary objectives, it uses various instruments to control commercial banks. Such instruments include: 

1. Open Market Operation (OMO): Open Market Operation involves buying and selling of financial securities from and to commercial bank so as to control (increase or reduce) the volume of money in circulation. When the money in circulation is high, the central bank sells these securities, which will reduce the commercial’s bank ability to give loans; thereby, reducing the quantity of money in circulation. On the other hand, when the amount of money in circulation is too low, the Central bank will buy securities from the public and commercial banks. When it makes payment to them, the money with the commercial banks will increase and they will increase their lending to their customers. This action will increase the money in circulation. 

2. Cash ratio (Liquidity ratio): Liquidity ratio is also known as cash reserve ratio. The Central bank requires all commercial banks to keep with it, certain percentage of their total deposits. If the Central bank wants to increase the amount of money in circulation during a period of deflation, it will lower the cash ratio and the amount of cash available to banks will increase. Therefore, they will be able to grant loans. But if the Central bank wants to reduce the amount of money in circulation may be during an inflationary period, it will increase the cash ratio and the lending ability of commercial banks will decrease. 

3. Use of bank rate: Bank rate also known as discount rate. It is the rate at which the Central bank lends money to commercial banks and other financial institutions. High bank rate will make borrowing costly and scare people away

4. Moral suasion: it involves persuasion from the Central bank to commercial bank without any form of force of law, on lending policies to be adopted. It is done on moral ground. 

5. Special deposit: this is an instruction from the Central Bank demanding special deposits from the commercial bank which is above their statutory requirement. The deposit is kept with the central Bank. This reduces the lending ability of the commercial bank.

6. Special Directives: these are special instructions from the Central bank to commercial bank and other financial institution about lending policies.

Ways in which the Central Bank Assists in Carrying Out Government Monetary Policy 

Monetary Policy is a policy set by the Central Bank that involves changing the interest rate and influencing the money supply. It can boost consumer spending through lower interest rates that makes borrowing cheaper on everything from credit cards to mortgage. 

We have two kinds of Monetary policy 

  1. Expansionary monetary policy
  2. Contractionary monetary policy

Expansionary monetary policy works by expanding the money supply faster than usual or lowering short-term interest rates. It is enacted by central banks and comes about through open market operations, reserve requirements, and setting interest rates.

Contractionary monetary policy is driven by increases in the various base interest rates controlled by modern central banks or other means producing growth in the money supply. The goal is to reduce inflation by limiting the amount of active money circulating in the economy.

The Central Bank Can assist in carrying out Government Monetary Policy by

1. Serving as banker to the government: The central bank keeps account for the government. It advises the government with regard to raising loans and helps with financial planning and budget. 

2. It serves as executive instrument for monetary policy. The tools used are: 

  1. Bank rate
  2. Open market operation
  3. Cash ratio/liquidity ratio
  4. Special deposit
  5. Funding
  6. Directives/selective credit control

Differences Between the Central and Commercial Banks 

1. There is only one central bank in any country that regulates the amount of money in the economy, while, there are many commercial banks in any economy. 

2. Central bank is established by an act of parliament. On the other hand, commercial banks are established through incorporation. 

3. Profit making is not the main aim of establishing the central bank, whereas, commercial banks are established for profit-making. 

4. The central bank is directly responsible to the government of the country while commercial banks are responsible to the shareholders. 

5. The central bank is owned by the central government. while, commercial banks are owned by the shareholders, organizations, governments or individuals. 

6. The central bank. does not accept deposits from individuals but keeps account of the government whereas commercial banks accept deposits and keep peoples? accounts and valuables. 

7. The central bank is the only authority that issues currencies in the economy while the commercial banks are not authorized to do so. 

8. The central bank keeps the foreign reserves of the country and also control the foreign exchange market, whereas, the commercial banks go through the central bank for its foreign exchange requirements.

Non-Banking Financial Institutions 

Development Banks 

Development banks are banks established for the main purpose of providing medium and long-term loans for capital projects in order to enhance economic development. They assist in development of such sectors like agriculture, commerce, industry etc. which are necessary for economic development. They “are usually owned by government or jointly with private organizations. A greater share is owned by government. Examples are Bank 'of Agriculture (BOA), Urban Development Bank of Nigeria P1c., Nigerian Bank for Commerce and Industry (NBCI), Nigerian Agriculture Co-operation and Rural Development Bank, Bank of Industry (BOI) etc.

Merchant Banks 

Merchant banks are financial institutions that provide specialized services like acceptance of bills of exchange, corporate finance, portfolio management, equipment leasing and acceptance of deposits in order to promote the growth of commerce and industry. They are called acceptance houses. Examples are Chase Merchant bank now Continental Merchant bank, Merchant Bank of Africa, etc. 

Insurance Companies 

Insurance companies are financial institutions that deal on insurance. They provide safeguards against risks of different kinds like fire, accident, burglary or theft, marine, untimely death etc. 

Insurance is a pool of risks and an agreement by the insured to pay premium to an insurance company (against any loss insured against). Their operations are grouped into two: 

  1. Life assurance which deals with event which must happen such as death.
  2. General insurance deals with probabilities, that is, events which may happen. They include motor vehicle insurance, fire insurance, insurance against accidents, marine, burglary insurance, fidelity guarantee. An example is Nigeria Social Insurance Trust Fund (NSITF).

The Stock Exchange

The stock exchange is a market for buying and selling of old securities such as shares, stocks, debentures and bond. It provides facilities for bringing the buyers and sellers of second-hand securities together, that is, securities that have already been in existence but on which the change of ownership is needed. The public does not Operate directly in the stock exchange but through the jobbers and the brokers. 

Financial Market

Financial market is a market for buying and selling of securities. Money and capital markets are types of financial market.

Money Market 

Money market is a financial market for obtaining short-term loans (under 1 year). The loans are obtained through the use of bill of exchange which includes trade bills and treasury bills. Financial instruments in money market include treasury bills, commercial paper, bill of exchange, treasury certificate, etc. Financial institutions that operate in the money market are discount houses, commercial banks, merchant banks, acceptance houses; finance companies, hire purchase companies; the central bank serves as the lender of last resort etc. 

Capital Market 

Capital market is a financial market for medium and long-tem loans. It is the major source of capital for investors and industrialists. Capital market is divided into two: 

(i) Primary market 

(ii)Secondary market.

Primary market deals with buying and selling of new securities. Secondary market deals with already existing (old) securities like shares, stocks, bonds. The loans given in capital market mature in two years and above. The principal participants that operate in capital market are the stock exchange, development banks, insurance companies, investment banks, saving banks, finance corporations, mortgage banks, investment trust, issuing houses etc. The financial instruments are stocks, shares, company bonds and government bonds. 

 

Types and Features of Capital Market Securities 

1. Bond: Bond is a long-term debt security in which an investor loans money to an entity (corporation or government) for a defined period of time at a fixed interest rate. It can be used by companies and government. Interest on bonds is paid every six months. 

2. Equity: Equity is also known as ‘shares’ or ‘stock’. It is a unit of investment in a company. It is used by companies to raise long-term loans in capital market. A company’s stockholders (shareholders) are co-owners of the company.

3. Development stocks: These are long-term debt instruments issued by Central Bank on behalf of federal government. They are used to raise long-term loans for developmental projects. 

4. Debenture: Debenture is a long-term debt instrument used by large companies to borrow money at a fixed rate of interest. Debenture holders are creditors of the company and not co-owners. 

NB: The capital market is a market where long-term loan are obtained and where buying and selling of shares equally take place. An example is the stock-exchange market. Joint stock companies require long-term capital shares, stocks, debentures, etc., which is offered in the capital market. The money market/ cash investments mean a financial market that specializes in the provision of short-term loans and advances.

Things You Should Remember About Central Bank

The Central bank can increase the amount of money in circulation by purchasing government securities from commercial banks and institutions. Buying the security frees up bank assets leading to more money in circulation.

Fiduciary issue means issuing of banknotes by a central bank without the backing in gold. This banknote has no gold value but only depends on the reputation of the Central Bank that issued it. 

Central Bank charges discount rate to commercial banks when they borrow reserves. This is used by the Central Bank as a monetary policy because a decrease in the discount rate encourages banks to borrow, which increases the amount of available reserves held by the banking system, which then induces an increase in the money supply and a decrease in interest rates. In the reverse case an increase in the discount rate works in the opposite direction, discouraging borrowing, reducing available reserves, decreasing the money supply, and increasing interest rates also.

The Central Bank is a financial Institution established by the government to manage the financial institutions as well as to control and supervise other financial institutions.

Central Bank is the only institution with the right of issuing currency in Nigeria. It controls other banks and maintain a stable price system.
