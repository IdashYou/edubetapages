1. PRICE SYSTEM AND DEMAND AND SUPPLY FUNCTIONS

The price system is a mechanism of resource allocation that relies upon monetary prices set by the interplay of forces of demand and supply. The resulting prices serve as signals to producers and consumers which guide production and distribution of resources. This chapter describes functions of price system, price determination, effects of changes in demand and supply on the equilibrium price and quantity. 

Functions of the Price System

  1. Price system is used in allocating resources among competing ends
  2. It determines the extent of demand and supply
  3. It indicates the direction of demand
  4. It facilitates exchange of goods and services
  5. It rewards owners of factors of production
  6. It discourages unnecessary pressure on some commodity

Price Determination in a Free Market Economy (Perfect Competition).

Equilibrium price and quantity are determined in a free market economy by the interaction of forces of demand and supply (called the market forces). 

Equilibrium price is the price at which the quantity demanded of a commodity is equal to the quantity supplied. This is called the market price. This illustrates the third law of demand and supply, which states that price tend to an equilibrium level at the point where the quantity demanded is equal to the quantity supplied. At this equilibrium price, there is no excess demand or supply. All that is supplied are sold and all that is demanded are bought.

Above the equilibrium price, suppliers will supply more to the market than the consumers demanded. Supply will therefore be greater than demand which is excess supply. Below the equilibrium price, consumers will demand more goods and services than suppliers are willing to supply. This will cause excess demand. Excess demand can in turn cause scarcity of the product. However, the forces of demand and supply (that is, the invisible hand as named by Adam Smith) will react in such a way as to bring the equilibrium price at the end. This equilibrium price is the price at which price is fairly stable except if there is changes in other factors affecting demand or supply. 

Laws of Demand and Supply 

  • The higher the price of a commodity, the lower the quantity demanded and the lower the price, the higher the quantity demanded.
  • The higher the price of a commodity, the higher the quantity supplied; the lower the price the lower the quantity supplied. 
  • Prices tend to an equilibrium level at the point where the quantity demanded is equal to the quantity supplied. 
  • An increase in demand brings about increase in equilibrium price and quantity; while a decrease in demand will cause a fall in equilibrium price and quantity. 
  • An increase in supply of a commodity will lead to a fall in the equilibrium price and a rise in the equilibrium quantity, while a decrease in supply will increase the equilibrium price and reduce the quantity. 

Price Control

Price control is a policy aimed at controlling the price of goods and services in the market. Government intervenes when there are market failures. The inability of the market forces of demand and supply to determine prices may bring about government laws to regulate prices through the price control. There are conditions which may require the use of price control. Some of these conditions are: 

i. Shortage of essential commodities due to import restriction, poor harvest due to flood or natural disaster etc. This situation may result in Continuous increase in prices. 

ii. Government’s expansionary monetary policy in a time of inelastic output supply such as war period. This may lead to outrageous increase in prices and the government or its agency may embark in price control measure. 

iii. A situation where some influential members in a community engage in collusion with some merchants to exploit consumers by fixing prices. 

These situations may require the attention of the government and its agencies to stabilize the prices through maximum price legislation and minimum price legislation. 

Maximum Price Legislation

The maximum price legislation is the intervention of the government in fixing the highest price of essential commodities by legislation (or what is called price ceiling). Maximum price legislation is used by the government to protect consumers against exploitation by sellers. It is set below the equilibrium price. 

Minimum Price Legislation 

This refers to the intervention of the government in fixing the lowest price at which some selected commodities could be bought or sold. It is sometimes called ‘price flooring’. Minimum price setting is used to encourage producers especially producers of agricultural products to continue production. It is also introduced in the payment of Wages to protect the employees; example is minimum wage.

Objectives of the Price Control Policy (Minimum and Maximum price legislation) 

1. Prevention of exploitation of the consumers by producers: Maximum price control is used to protect the consumers from unscrupulous producers who usually increase the price of their commodities without increasing the quality in order to make excess profits. 

2. It is used to avoid or control inflation: Maximum legislation is used to control inflation. The aim is to avoid continuous increase in price of commodities which may lead to inflation. Where inflation is already in the economy, government can use maximum price legislation to stop further increase in the price of commodities. 

3. To help low income earners: One of the objectives of price control is to help low income earners to earn income that will provide the basic necessities of life. An example is the minimum wage. 

4. It is used control the profits of companies: Excess profits can cause monopoly if it is not controlled Maximum price legislation is Sea in this situation. 

5. It is used prevent fluctuation of prices of some products: This is aimed at stabilizing the prices of some products especially agricultural products. Minimum price legislation 1 is appropriate for this purpose. 

6. It is used stabilize the income of some producers: If the prices of agricultural products are prevented from fluctuation, then the incomes of farmers will be stabilized. This will encourage them to go on producing. 

7. It is used make possible planning for future output: Minimum price legislation ensures relative stability in income and makes it possible for producers (especially agricultural producers) to plan for future production. 

Meaning of Subsidy: Subsidy is a payment made by government to producers in order to keep down prices of certain commodities at desired level. 

Example is Nigerian government subsidy on petroleum which it is gradually withdrawing. 

Demand and Supply Functions Calculation

Equilibrium price is the price at which the quantity demanded is equal to the quantity supplied. This implies that Qd = Qs. Therefore; quantity demanded, quantity supplied and equilibrium price can be worked out with simultaneous equation. 

Steps involved

  1. Equate the demand and supply function to form at simultaneous equation.
  2. Collect like terms together and solve to get the equilibrium price.
  3. Substitute the price in any of the equations to get the equilibrium quantity (Qd or Qs).
