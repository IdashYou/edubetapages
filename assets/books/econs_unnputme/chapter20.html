20. NATIONAL INCOME

National Income (N.I.)

National income is the monetary value of all goods and services produced in a country during a period one year. 

This is equivalent to total incomes earned by four factors of production such as rent, wages, profits and interest within a year. It can also be the aggregate of all personal incomes. 

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

It means the total value of the goods and services produced by the people of a nation during a year not including the value of income earned in foreign countries

It can be defined as the monetary value of all goods and services produced within a country by all the residents of the country in a year irrespective of whether they are citizens or foreigners, It does not include the, earnings of citizens from abroad. Allowances for depreciation are not made in GDP calculation. GDP is the same as Gross Domestic Income. It is measured at factor cost or market prices. 

GDP is the most commonly measured aspect of national income in developing countries because it is not easy for them to calculate the value of outputs of citizens abroad and the value of depreciation in the whole economy. 

Thus GDP = EQm 

Where, E = summation, Qm = market value of total output. 

Gross National Product (GNP)

It is the monetary value of all final goods and services produced by the

Citizens of a country in a year plus the net Income from abroad. Net income from abroad means the earnings of the citizens from other countries. Allowances are not made for depreciation. 

GNP is recommended by United Nation Organization (UNO) to be used for statistical purposes for international comparisons. GNP is obtained by adding GDP to the incomes of citizens abroad and subtracts the incomes of foreigners within the country. 

Thus GNP = GDP + (X - M) 

Where, X = incomes of citizens in other countries 

M = Incomes of foreigners in the country. 

GNP is also measured at factor cost or market prices. 

Net National Product (NNP)

It is the monetary value of all goods and services produced by all the citizens of a country in a year minus depreciation. In a short form, it is GNP less depreciation.

Thus, NNP = GNP – Depreciation. 

Personal Income

Personal income is the amount of money received by individuals or households over a given period of time. It includes rent, profits, interest and wages. 

Disposable Income

This is the amount of money that is left after payment of personal income tax. 

Methods of Measuring National Income

There are three ways of measuring national income. The national income of a country is usually estimated using three approaches which are the expenditure, income and output. The expenditure accounts for the total amount spent on consumption and investment purposes during a year. Income includes the summation of all incomes received by all the factors of production. The output accounts for the total money value of all goods and services produced in the country in the specified period of time.

The output approach to measuring national income is based on the computation of final output which is the total value of all goods and services a nation produces and thereby avoiding the problem of double counting.

1. Income Approach 

2. Output or Net Product Approach 

3. Expenditure Approach. 

Any of these three methods used by a country will give the same result. 

 

National Income = National product = National Expenditure. 

This equation means that when goods and services are produced by factors of production (land, labour, capital and entrepreneur), the factors are paid incomes in form of rent, wages, interest and profits. The total incomes received by these factors are called National Income. The value of the output produced by these factors must be equal to the incomes paid to them. 

The total value of goods and services produced by the factors are called National product or output. Again, the incomes received by the factors of production must be equal to the expenditure on output in form of consumption and investment. 

Therefore, the total sum of all expenditure on output in a country is called National expenditure. 

Income approach

This approach measures all incomes earned by the various factors of production (land, labour, capital and entrepreneur) in the form of wages, interest, rent and profits. It measures the national income at factor cost. Symbolically, 

GNP = W + l + R + P + Depreciation + tax. 

Where, W = wage, I = interest, R = rent, p = profits. 

To avoid double-counting, transfer payment in the form of gifts donations, pensions, gratuities etc. are not included. Business expenses are excluded. 

This method is widely used in West Africa. 

Output or net product approach

This approach measures the total monetary value of all goods and services produced by various sectors of the economy. Here, the value of net contribution of each sector is added. Therefore, to avoid double-counting, income is measured on a value-added basis. Value added basis is a technique used to measure the worth or value each sector contributes to the production of a product. Value added is the value of output minus cost of inputs. Only final product or value added should be considered. Moreover, the values " of exports’ are included while excluding the value of imports. The value of owner-occupied houses, output consumed by the producer, services of voluntary organization etc. are included. 

Symbolically, GNP = Pi x Qi.

Where, 

Pi = Price of the commodity 

Qi = Quantity of that commodity 

This approach measures national income at market prices. 

Expenditure approach

This method measures the total expenditure on consumption and investment made by individuals, firms and government on currently produced final goods and services during the year. 

To avoid double-counting, only expenditure on final goods and services are included. Expenses on goods and services used in production (inputs) are excluded. 

Symbolically, 

GNP or Y = C + 1 + G + (X - M) 

Where, 

Y = National Income, C = Private consumption expenditure.

I = Private investment Expenditure, G = Government expenditure on consumption and Investment 

X = Exports, M = Imports 

Problems of Computing National Income 

Many problems are encountered in the measurement of national income. These problems make national income figures to be over-estimated or under-estimated. They are as follows: , 

1. Double-counting

2. Inadequate data

3. Unqualified services

4. Subsistence production

5. Changes in the value of money

6. Valuation depreciation on capital stock

7. Owner-occupied houses

8. Illegal transactions
