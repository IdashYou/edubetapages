1. THEORY OF DEMAND

Meaning of Demand 

Demand is the quantity of a commodity which consumers are willing and able to buy at any given time and at a particular price. This means that before we can talk of demand, the consumers must have the purchasing power (that is money) and must be willing to exchange it with goods and services. It is worthy to note that demand is different from want and need. 

Want 

Want is a wish or mere desire to have goods and services. The person may not have the money or may not be willing to exchange his/her money. 

Need 

Need is an urgent requirement for an essential commodity but there is no ability and willingness to pay. Wants and needs become demand when they are backed up with the ability and willingness to pay otherwise called effective demand. 

Effective Demand 

Effective demand is a desire, or need that is backed up with the ability and willingness to pay the prevailing price. 

Demand Schedule 

Demand schedule is a table showing the quantities of a good or service that is demanded at different prices. It shows the relationship between the different prices of the commodity and the quantities demanded of such commodity. It is of two types: 

(a) Individual demand schedule 

(b) Market demand schedule 

An individual demand schedule is a table which shows the quantities of each commodity demanded by an individual at different prices. 

Market demand schedule is a table which shows the overall or aggregate quantities of a commodity that consumers are willing to pay at various prices.

Demand Curve 

Demand curve is a graphical representation of the relationship between the price of a product and the quantity of that product which consumers are willing to-buy at that price. A normal demand curve slopes downwards from left to right showing that demand increase with a fall in price and decreases with a rise in price. It has a negative slope. In plotting the demand curve, the price is plotted at the Y-axis while the quantity demanded on the X-axis. It is important to note that in drawing a demand curve, it is assumed that price is the only variable affecting demand while all other factors are held constant. It is of two types: individual demand curve and market demand curve: We are going to draw an individual demand curve using the individual schedule.


 

Abnormal or Exceptional Demand Curve

Abnormal demand curve is a demand curve which does not slope downwards from left to right. It does not have a negative slope and does not follow the first law of demand. In this case, sometimes, a fall in the price of a commodity may not bring about an increase in the quantity demanded of such commodity. Rather, an increase in the price of the commodity will bring about increase in the quantity demanded of such commodity. In other words, the demand curve will have a positive slope. 

An abnormal demand curve is said to be regressive or backward sloping. 

Reasons for Abnormal Demand Curve 

1. Perfectly inelastic demand: -Essential goods that have no close substitute like common salt have abnormal demand curve. The demand curve is perfectly inelastic. A change in the price of the commodity does not bring about any change in quantity demanded. In other words, whether the price increases or decreases, the quantity [demanded of it remains the same. 

2. Inferior or ‘Giffen Goods’ 

Inferior goods are commodities whose demand decreases as income increases. They are usually consumed by the poor because they cannot afford the superior substitutes. But when their incomes increase, even it the price of such commodity falls, the consumers will demand less of it. At this point, they use the new incomes to purchase superior goods. This makes the demand curve to be regressive at the base showing that less of the inferior good is demanded as its price decreases. Example of inferior goods is second hand clothes. 

3. Luxury goods (Ostentatious) : 

A luxury good (or upmarket good) is a good for which demand increases more than proportionally as income rises, so that expenditures on the good become a greater proportion of overall spending. Luxury goods are associated with self-esteem, uniqueness, Quality, materialism, Fashion and investment. They are consumed by the rich people in order to show off their wealth. As their prices increase, people' think their values have increased and therefore buy more of them. This causes the demand curve to slope upwards showing that more will be bought at higher prices. Examples are luxurious cars, Jewelries (gold, diamond etc.).

4. Speculation: 

Expectation of future rise or fall in price can cause abnormal demand curve. If people are expecting that price will rise in future, even if the price increases at the moment, they will buy more of that commodity, thereby causing increase in demand. On the other hand, if consumers are speculating that the price of a particular commodity will fall in future, even When the price of such commodity decreases at the moment, they will buy less, waiting for more decrease in price of such commodity. 

5. Quality-related prices:

The quality of some commodities is determined by their prices, such commodities have abnormal demand curve. The higher the prices of such commodities, the' higher 'their qualities as believed by consumers. More of such commodities will therefore be bought at higher prices, examples, wine, mobile phones etc. 

6. Valuable antiques or rare commodities:

Valuable belongings to the distant past or rare commodities have abnormal demand curves. When their prices increase, consumers buy more, however, if the prices fall, their demand is normal. It has two slopes. 

7. Perfectly elastic demand: 

Here, a change in price will, bring about a big change in quantity demanded. If price is increased a little, consumers will stop buying the good but if price is reduced a little, they may buy all the commodities, all things being equal.

Price elasticity of demand (PED)  

PED is a measure of the sensitivity of the quantity variable, Q, to changes in the price variable, P. Elasticity answers the question of how much the quantity will change in percentage terms for a 1% change in the price, and is thus important in determining how revenue will change. PED is negative because of the inverse relationship between the price of a good and the quantity of the good demanded, a consequence of the law of demand.

The elasticity of demand indicates how sensitive the demand for a good is to a price change. If the absolute value of PED is between zero and 1, demand is said to be inelastic; if the absolute value of PED equals 1, the demand is unitary elastic; and if the absolute value of Price elasticity of demand is greater than 1, demand is elastic. A low coefficient implies that changes in price have little influence on demand. A high elasticity indicates that consumers will respond to a price rise by buying a lot less of the good and that consumers will respond to a price cut by buying a lot more...

Taxes and subsidies

A sales tax on the commodity does not directly change the demand curve, if the price axis in the graph represents the price including tax. Similarly, a subsidy on the commodity does not directly change the demand curve, if the price axis in the graph represents the price after deduction of the subsidy.

If the price axis in the graph represents the price before addition of tax and/or subtraction of subsidy then the demand curve moves inward when a tax is introduced, and outward when a subsidy is introduced.

Factors that affect Demand for a commodity 

  1. The price of the commodity
  2. The prices of other commodities
  3. The preferences of the consumer
  4. The income of the consumer
  5. Government Policy
  6. Population
  7. Fashion

Factors affecting market demand

In addition to the factors which can affect individual demand there are three factors that can cause the market demand curve to shift:

  1. a change in the number of consumers,
  2. a change in the distribution of tastes among consumers,
  3. a change in the distribution of income among consumers with different tastes.

Some circumstances which can cause the demand curve to shift in include:

  1. Decrease in price of a substitute
  2. Increase in price of a complement
  3. Decrease in income if good is normal good
  4. Increase in income if good is inferior good

Types of Demand

There are four types of demand namely Competitive Demand, Joint or Complementary Demand, Composite Demand and Derived Demand. Demand is the amount of a product buyers are willing and able to purchase at a given price over a particular period of time.

a. Competitive Demand

Commodities are substitutes if one can be used in place of the other. Substitute goods serve the same purpose and therefore compete for the consumers’ income. They are said to have competitive demand because of the fact that they compete for the consumers’ income. Examples of substitute goods are Milo and bournvita, butter and margarine and others. A change in the price of one affects the demand for the other. If for instance there is an increase in the price of butter, demand for margarine will increase which will ultimately increase the price of margarine, provided the supply of margarine does not change. On the other hand, a decrease in the price of butter will lead to a decrease in the demand for margarine, and hence a fall in its price, given the supply.

b. Joint or Complementary Demand

Two or more goods are said to be jointly demanded when they must be consumed together to provide a given level of satisfaction. Some examples are cars and fuel, compact disc players and CD. There are perfect complementary goods and imperfect or poor complementary goods. For perfect complementary goods, the consumer practically cannot do without the other. An example is cars and fuel. On the other hand, for imperfect complementary goods, a consumer can do without the other, so long as a substitute is obtained. For complementary demand, a change in the price of one good affect the demand for the other. If there should be an increase in the price of compact disc players, there will be a decrease in the demand for discs, other things being equal.

c. Derived Demand

When the demand for a commodity is derived from the demand for the final commodity, that commodity is said to have derived demand. Wood may be demanded for the purpose of manufacturing furniture and not for its own sake. Here, the demand for wood is derived from the demand for furniture. Demand for wood is therefore a derived demand.

Factors of production such as land, labor, and capital have derived demand. This is because an increase in the demand for a commodity will result in an increase in the factors of production used in producing the goods. The price of the factors of production will increase, other things being equal.

d. Composite Demand

Composite demand applies to commodities which have several uses or are demanded for several and different purposes. Wood as mentioned in the example above is used for furniture – tables, chairs, beds, windows, doors and others. A change in demand for one of them will affect all others. If there is an increase in demand for table this will result in higher prices being paid for wood. The high price for wood will increase the cost of production of chairs, bed, windows and doors and any other thing for which wood is used in manufacturing
