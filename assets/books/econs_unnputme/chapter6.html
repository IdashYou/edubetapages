6. THEORY OF SUPPLY 

Supply is the quantity of goods and services which the sellers are willing and able to offer for sale at a particular price and at a given period of time. An example is when a producer produces 50 loaves of bread, keeps 10 loaves, consumes 5 loaves with his family and brings 35 loaves to the market for sale at the prevailing market price. Supply is 35 loaves of bread, not the 50 loaves that was produced.

Supply Schedule 

A supply schedule is a table which shows the amount of a commodity that was offered for sale at various prices. It is of two types: the individual supply schedule and market supply schedule. 

Individual supply schedule is a table showing different quantities of goods and services that was offered for sale at the prevailing market price b a single seller.

Market supply schedule is a table which shows the overall quantities of commodity which all the sellers offered for sale at different prices. It is the summation of individual supply schedules of a particular commodity.

Supply Curve

supply curve is a graphical representation of the relationship between the price of a commodity and the quantity supplied of that commodity. It is therefore a graphical representation of a supply schedule. A normal supply curve slopes Upward from left to right. In other word, it has a positive slope indicating that an increase in price will lead to an increase in quantity supplied and vice versa. Abnormal Supply Curves 

Abnormal supply curves do not follow the law of supply. It does not slope upwards from left to right. In other words, it does not have a positive slope. It can be seen as limitations to the law of supply. It is a situation whereby increase in the price of the commodity does not lead to an increase in the supply of that commodity. 

Cases of Abnormal Supply Curve 

1. Fixed supply curve: This is a situation where change in price does not affect change in the supply of the product. Land is an example of this abnormal case, the supply curve is parallel to the price axis showing that at various prices, the same quantity is supplied. 

Circumstances that can lead to fixed supply curves are as follows: 

a. Long run supply of land: In the long-run, supply of land is fixed. The supply of land can. be increased in the short-run by land reclamation, irrigation and through the application of fertilizer and manure to improve land fertility. However, this cannot continue because land has diminishing return. Supply of mineral resources is fixed in the long-run since you cannot continue to mine it indefinitely. 

b. Short-run supply of agricultural products: If there is increase in the price of agricultural products, such as, cotton, groundnut etc., within the short-run period, it is not easy to increase the supply because it is seasonal. It has to take a long period, may be another harvesting season to produce more of the products. Agricultural products are not ordinary goods you can produce immediately and supply to the market no matter the price increase. 

 

2. A supply curve with more than one slope: Rising income is another cause of abnormal supply curve. It was discovered that workers are ready to increase the number of working hours at a higher wage rate in order to earn more. But as the wage rate continues to increase, workers begin to reduce the number of working hours, thereby reducing supply of labour. They begin to realize that they need leisure time to enjoy their incomes. They begin to trade incomes with leisure. The supply curve is. backward sloping. Another reason for this is risks posed to life. 

3. Expectation of future change in price by sellers: If Sellers have the fear that price of commodity will rise in future, they will sell less at a higher price waiting for the price increase to make more profits. On the other hand, if they are expecting a fall it price, they will sell more even at a lower price to avoid future loss. In that case, the supply curve will be negatively sloped. 

The Law of Supply 

The law of supply states that more goods and services will be offered for sale at a higher price and less at a lower price. 

Difference between change in supply and change in quantity supplied 

1. Change in Supply


Change in supply is an increase or decrease in supply as a result of changes in factors affecting supply other than the price of the commodity. It involves a complete shift of the supply curve rightward or leftward as the case may be. It entails higher or lower quantity supplied at a constant price. Change in supply can be increase or decrease. 

i. Increase in supply: 

Here the supply curve shifts bodily outward or to the right showing that more of the commodity will be supplied at the same price. 

ii. Decrease in Supply: 

There is a decrease in supply when the quantity supplied decreases as a result of unfavorable change in factors affecting supply other than price. The supply curve shifts bodily inward or to the left. 

2. Change in Quantity supplied 

Change in quantity supplied is an increase or decrease in quantity supplied of a commodity caused solely by changes in the price of the commodity. It involves a movement along the same supply curve. It can be increase or decrease in quantity supplied.


i. Increase in quantity supplied:

This occurs when an increase in price brings about increase in the quantity supplied. It can be called expansion of supply.

ii. Decrease in Quantity Supplied: 

There is a decrease in quantity applied if the quantity supplied falls owing to a decrease in price. It is referred to as a contraction of supply.

 

Factors Affecting Changes in Supply

  1. Changes in price of other goods
  2. Changes in technology
  3. Changes in the cost of production
  4. Changes in the number of producers
  5. Changes in weather
  6. Changes in government policy
