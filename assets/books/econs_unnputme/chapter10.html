10. PRODUCTION

Meaning of Production 

Production is defined as the creation of utilities or goods and services to satisfy human wants. Utility means satisfaction of human wants. Examples of production include manufacturing of cloth from cotton, teaching service, provision of hospital services

Production is not complete till it reaches the final consumer and satisfy human wants.

Types of Goods 

There are two types of goods: Capital goods and consumer goods. 

Capital goods are goods used in production of other goods. Examples are machineries, tools, raw materials, company vehicles etc. These goods are not needed for their own sake but they are useful in the production of other goods. 

Consumer goods are goods which are required for direct consumption in satisfaction of wants. They do not need further processing. Examples are foods, shoes, clothes, car etc. 

We also have a category of goods called the invisible goods. Invisiblegoods refer to those items which cannot be seen, felt, touched or measured. Examples of invisible items include international travel and consulting services offered in a different country. invisible items is also known as services rendered

Types of Production 

Two forms of Production exist which are direct and indirect production.

Direct Production: Direct production is subsistent in nature it takes place when a family unit produces goods and services mainly to satisfy all its needs and he does not need exchange of goods and services. This type of production is very common in a subsistence economy where subsistence farming is predominant. It does not involve commercial agriculture and therefore have no need for mechanization and it’s not for sale.

Indirect Production: Indirect production is commercial in nature and involves the production of goods and services mainly for exchange in order to use the money realised to satisfy the producer’s other wants. This type of production takes place because, it is not possible in the modern society for anybody to practice subsistence lifestyle. In modern industrial economy, the society has become inter-dependent. People satisfy their wants indirectly by exchanging their surplus, outputs with other producers or selling their goods and services and using the money realised in satisfying their numerous wants through exchange. 

Stages of Production 

Production involves 3 categories of economic activities.

1. Primary Production: this involves acquiring raw materials from their source. For example, metals and coal have to be mined, oil drilled from the ground, rubber tapped from trees, foodstuffs farmed and fish trawled. This is sometimes known as extractive production. This type of production involves the extractive industry where the occupation of people is directed to the process of extracting raw materials from the soil or sea. The goods and services produced at this first stage of production are known as raw materials. Examples of this type of production are agriculture, forestry, fishing, mining and oil extraction.

2. Secondary Production: This is a stage that involves the processing of the extracted raw materials into finished goods. This stage adds utility to the raw materials making them useful. The manufacturing and constructive industries are involved in this stage of production. It involves converting raw materials into components, for example, making plastics from oil. It also involves assembling the product, eg building houses, bridges and roads.

3. Tertiary Production: this refers to the commercial services that support the production and distribution process, e.g. insurance, transport, advertising, warehousing and other services such as teaching and health care. There is need for those goods and services produced in the secondary production to reach the end users effectively through these commercial services. This stage completes the production processes since production is not complete until the goods and services produced reach the final consumers. 

Tertiary production is the creation of intangible goods, e.g. distribution, communication, banking, insurance, tourism. teaching, legal services etc. 

Intangible goods: An intangible good is a good that does not have a physical nature, as opposed to a physical good (an object).

Factors that Determine the Volume of Production

1. The quantity and quality of factors of Production: The factors of production (land, capital, labour and entrepreneur) are the resources used in creating or manufacturing a good or service in an economy. Their abundance and good quality will lead to higher productivity. The more the labourers the more the products produced. 

2. The size of Market: Market size is the number of individuals in a certain market segment who are potential buyers. Large market will demand for more production of services.

3. The nature of the product: perishable goods are produced based on the current demand of the product, since there might be losses due to spoilage or preservation. For e.g. a tomato farmer may suffer loss if he produces more products than what is demanded in the market, because the tomato will either spoil or he spends extra money in storing them. A non-perishable good can be mass produced and stored in warehouse.  

4. Storage facilities: the presence of good storage facility will encourage large volume of production, since they can be stored until they are needed. 

5. Government Policy: good government Policy will make it easier for companies to produce in large quantities. The more favorable the policy is to producers the more the production. 

6. Availability of Raw Materials: the more available the raw materials are the more the production of goods. Raw Materials is a big determinant of the volume of production. 

7. Attitude of workers: positive attitude of workers will lead to the production of more goods and services and vice versa.

8. The level of Technology: the more efficient and stable the machine is the more the production. 

9. Division of Labour: the more specialized the workers are in their duty, the greater the volume of product that will be produced since it will save time and energy.

10. The Management: effective management is very important in Production. The more effective the management is the more the volume of goods produced. 

Factors of Production 

Factors of production are resources or inputs which are coordinated to produce goods and services. There are four factors of production. They include land, labour, capital and entrepreneur. 

Land 

Land is a free gift of nature that is fixed in supply such as water, forest, mineral resources, soil etc. The reward for land is rent. 

Features or Quality of Land 

1. Land is a free gift of nature; it is not man-made. 

2. The supply of land is fixed

3. Land is subjected to the law of diminishing returns: Diminishing returns occur on land because of its fixed nature. If extra unit of labour and capital are applied to a fixed factor which is land, at a stage, the marginal output will start to decrease. 

4. Land is geographically immobile

5. Land is heterogeneous in nature: Some lands are more fertile than others. 

6. Land is subject to the law of diminishing return.

7. the reward of land is rent

Labour 

Labour refers to man’s physical and mental effort directed towards production of goods and services. Examples of physical efforts are the services of masons, farmers, production workers etc. While the mental efforts are the services of teachers, lawyers, accountants, doctors etc. The quantity of labour is dependent on. the total supply of labour while quality of labour determines its efficiency. The reward of labour is wages or salary. 

Characteristics of Labour

  1. Labour is very mobile
  2. Labour controls other factors of production
  3. Labour is a human factor of production and thus unpredictable.
  4. Labour is perishable with time
  5. Labour can develop from unskilled to skilled labour.
  6. Labour is not a fixed factor of production, its quantity can be increased through acquisition of skills and procreation.
  7. The reward for labour is wages or salary.

Types of Labour

Skilled Labour: these are workers with higher education like University level, who involve more of their mental efforts in carrying out production process. They are specially trained in what they do and carries out administrative and managerial positions like accountancy, lawyers, Doctors etc.

Unskilled Labour: this are labourers who possesses little or no education, and uses more of their physical strength and energy to participate in the production process, for example a cleaner in the company.

Semi-Skilled Labour: this is the labour that involves the combination of both skilled and unskilled effort in the production process, they are partly educated, for example a typist who possesses only WAEC certificate.

Capital 

Capital is man-made resources used to produce other goods and services. In other word, it is wealth set aside for the production of further wealth. They are called ‘investment goods’ or capital goods’ because they are used to produce consumer goods. Examples of capital are machines, tools, equipment, money, raw materials, fuel etc. Capital is a passives factor. The reward for capital is interest. 

Characteristics of Capital

  1. Capital is Geographically immobile
  2. Capital is a man-made factor of production
  3. Accumulates through savings
  4. It is subject to depression.
  5. The reward of capital is interest, but some capital may be fixed as in a building functioning as capital and in such case can be rewarded as rent.

Types of Capital

We have three types of capital, Fixed Capital, Circulating Capital, Current Capital and Social Capital.

1. Fixed Capital: these are those assets of a business or productive unit that are durable and does not change their form during production process. Examples of fixed Assets are buildings, machinery, furniture, etc.

2. Circulating Capital (Working capital): another name for circulating capital is working capital which refer to those materials that change their form in the process of production and they are needed for daily running of the business (e.g. raw materials).

3. Current capital (Liquid capital): another name for current capital is Liquid capital which refers to the items needed for day to day running of the business. They are also knownt o change from one form to another. Money is an example of current capital which changes form when it is used to buy raw materials or paying wages and salaries.

4. Social Capital (social Ameenities): these are capital provided by government in the form of social amenities that aids production. Examples of these social amenities includes; electricity, roads, airports, airports etc.

Entrepreneur

Entrepreneur is the managerial services applied to co-ordinate other factors of production. He is a person who co-ordinates, controls and organizes the production process. He combines and organizes land, labour and capital to obtain maximum output at minimum costs, with a view of making profit. He takes major decisions. He is the owner of the business and provides capital. He brings innovation on new products and new techniques of production. In other words, he is a risk bearer. The reward is profit. 

Characteristics of an Entrepreneur 

  1. Risk bearer
  2. Decision maker
  3. Coordinator
  4. Receives profit.
  5. He organizes research
  6. Miscellaneous functions
  7. He provides capital

The Law of Diminishing Returns

The law states that “ as more and more units of variable factors of production (such as labour and capital) are combined with the fixed of production (such as land), production will increase up to certain point when decrease in total output will start as a result of continuous addition of variable factors while the fixed factors remains constant. Law of diminishing returns explains that when more and more units of a variable input are employed on a given quantity of fixed inputs, the total output may initially increase at increasing rate and then at a constant rate, but it will eventually increase at diminishing rates. The law of diminishing returns operates in the short run when we cannot change all the factors of production. Further, it studies the change in output by varying the quantity of one (1) input. 

This law applies both to fixed and variable factors of production.

The stages of diminishing returns

When all the condition is met, meaning that one input varies while all others stay the same, the production will pass through these three stages according to the law of diminishing returns, these stages includes;

Stage 1: Increasing returns

Initially, adding to one production variable is likely to improve the output, as the fixed inputs are in abundance compared to the variable one. Therefore, adding more units of the variable factor will use the fixed factors more efficiently and increase production.

Stage 2: Diminishing returns

As more units of the variable factor are added, the overall production will continue to increase. However, during this stage, the total product increases at a continuously decreasing rate. This process culminates with the product reaching its maximum value, meaning that the marginal product becomes zero. Optimum production is set somewhere within this stage. Adding more units of the variable factor after this point will lead to the overall output starting to diminish.

Stage 3: Negative returns

Excessively adding to the variable input after the point of optimum production will eventually lead not only to a decrease in efficiency but even to a negative return of production. The excess in the variable factor now hurts the whole production process.

Relationship Between Total Product (TP), Average Product (AP) and Marginal Product (MP).

1. When total product is increasing, Marginal Product (MP) is also increasing, but must be below the Total Product (TP).

2. When Total Product (TP) is maximized, Marginal Product (MP) is Zero (0).

3. During decline in Total Product (TP), Marginal Product will be Negative.
