ELECTROLYSIS 

Electrolysis is a type of chemical reaction in which chemical decomposition of a compound takes place when an electric current is passed through the solution or molten form of the compound.

Electrolyte: this a compound either in solution or molten form that conducts electric current and in the process is decomposed at the electrodes. Moreover, a non-electrolyte is the opposite. Electrolytes vary in their ability to conduct electric current. We have strong and weak electrolytes.

Strong electrolytes are substances that ionizes completely during electrolysis, and they conduct electricity. Example NaCl.

Weak electrolytes are slightly ionized, thereby do not conduct electricity readily. Example is NH4OH and organic acids like tartaric acids, ethanoic acids and citric acids.

Non-electrolytes do not conduct because they don’t ionize. They are mostly covalent and organic compounds.  

Electrodes: these are conductors through which electric current enters or leaves the electrolyte during electrolysis. We have two types of electrodes, anodes and cathode

Anode: an anode is a positive electrode from which electric current enters the electrolyte during electrolysis. Tartaric acids, ethanoic acids and citric acids.

Cathode: a cathode is a negative electrode from which electric current leaves the electrolyte. It also means the electrode from which electron enters the electrolyte. 

Electrolytic cell: this is a chemical system consisting of container containing electrolyte with both anode and cathode connected to a suitable direct current supply.

In both electrolytic and electrochemical cell oxidation occurs at the anode while reduction occurs at the cathode. And electrons always flow from the anode to the cathode. The anode is the positive electrode which is connected to the positive terminal of the electric source. It is the electrode through which electrons leave the electrolyte. Loss of electron is oxidation.

Ionization

Ionization is a process of dissociation of a compound into ions. Ionization occurs before electric current is passed into electrolyte. Ions are known to move about in random motion to all direction. 

When an electric current is passed the random motion of an ion ceases, the positive ions are attracted to the cathode (negative electrode) and are known as cathode ions (cations), while the negative ions are attracted to the anode (positive electrode) and are known as Anode ions (anions).

Factors that determine Preferential Discharge of ions during Electrolysis

1. The Position of the ions in the electrochemical series 

2. The concentration of the ions

3. the nature of the electrodes

Applications of Electrolysis

Some metals, can be extracted only from electrolysis. These are usually very reactive metals like Sodium, Potassium, Calcium, Aluminium and magnesium. Less reactive metals like Zn, Fe, Pb, Cu are extracted by reduction by carbon or carbon monoxide.
