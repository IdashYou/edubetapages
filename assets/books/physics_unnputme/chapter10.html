Concept of Field

Field, In physics, a region in which each point is affected by a force. Objects fall to the ground because they are affected by the force of earth’s gravitational field (see gravitation). A paper clip, placed in the magnetic field surrounding a magnet, is pulled toward the magnet, and two like magnetic poles repel each other when one is placed in the other’s magnetic field. An electric field surrounds an electric charge; when another charged particle is placed in that region, it experiences an electric force that either attracts or repels it. The strength of a field, or the forces in a particular region, can be represented by field lines; the closer the lines, the stronger the forces in that part of the field. See also electromagnetic field.

 

Field can be seen as force acting at distance

 

A field is an imaginary concept, or you could say a mathematical concept, postulated because we have no better explanation. In the old days, when something like magnetism had an effect that didn’t involve matter “touching” something, but was able to affect matter over a limited range (a field of effect), it was called a field.

In the post-Renaissance scientific era of the 17th century, the law of gravity was postulated by Newton, who was troubled by its quality of “action at a distance.” We now call it a field, but this word does not appear in his Principia (searchable text at Full text of "Newton's Principia : the mathematical principles of natural philosophy" )

Later in the 19th century it was discovered that light (and radio waves) were vibrations or “excitations” in the “electromagnetic field.” Electric and magnetic fields were found to be related, and by the 1860s their mathematical description was complete except for quantum effects (Maxwell’s equations are even relativistically correct).

In the early 20th century it was discovered that matter was really tiny bits of stuff held together by fields, mostly the electromagnetic field, but within the nucleus 2 other kinds of fields.

As quantum theory developed, in which particles did not have always particular locations or states, it was difficult to continue calling the little bits of stuff “particles.” Finally quantum field theory (QFT) postulated that everything is a field. There is an “electron field” for example, and a particular electron is a packet of vibrations in that field. (QFT’s insight solves the problem of an electron going through two slits, but it does not solve the problem of explaining why a photon wave packet is absorbed at one particular location, the measurement problem).

So in physics today, a field is an imaginary space-filling idea that vibrates if you add energy to it.

Classification of field

A force field is classified into two; namely Scalar field and Vector field.

A Scalar field has only magnitude and no direction e.g. density, mass, temperature, etc.

A Vector field is a field that has both magnitude and direction e.g. magnetic field, electric field, and gravitational field.

 

Magnetism

When a bar magnet is placed near a magnetic material, they are usually attracted to the magnet due to magnetic force existing around the magnet. The magnetic force is present in the magnetic field surrounding the magnet.

When iron fillings are sprinkled on a bar magnet, the pattern shown by iron fillings indicates the direction of field surrounding the magnet. It is observed that the fillings are concentrated at the ends of the magnets which are called poles.

iron fillings sprinkled on a bar magnet

The poles of a magnet are the points or ends of a magnet where the attraction power of a magnet is concentrated.

There are two types of poles in a magnet and they are called the North-seeking pole (North pole) and South-seeking pole (South pole).

Law of Magnetism

When two bar magnets are brought close to each other, there is attraction or repulsion between the two magnets depending on the arrangement of the poles of the magnets. Attraction takes place when unlike poles face each other. While repulsion takes place when like poles face each other.

This observation led to the law of magnetism which states that like poles repel, unlike poles attract.

 

Introduction – Gravitational Field

It is common experience that when objects are thrown into the air, they move to the maximum height, stop momentarily and later fall back to the ground. The movement of objects up and down is due to the influence of gravitation on an object on the earth’s surface.

For every object that has mass is always surrounded by a gravitation field. A gravitation field is a region under the influence of gravitational force, it is a force field.

Acceleration due to gravity is the acceleration on an object caused by the force of gravitation; Neglecting air resistance, all small bodies accelerate in a gravitational field at the same rate in relation to centre of mass. At the surface of the earth, acceleration due to gravity is about 9.8ms-2.

Acceleration due to gravity is uniform at a particular place but varies from one part of the earth to another e.g. At the poles, acceleration due to gravity is more because of short distance compared to the large distance at the equator, hence when the distance between two objects (earth and other objects) increases, the force of attraction decreases. It is usually represented by g.

When a stone and a feather are dropped from a height, the two bodies are expected to touch the ground at the same time neglecting air resistance since they fall to the ground with the same acceleration.

In the practical sense, When  a stone and a feather are dropped from the same height at the same time, the stone falls at a faster speed until it reaches the ground ahead of the feather. The feather falls slowly due to air friction or resistance which reduces the motion of the feather than that of the stone. Also, due to the large surface area of the feather , the feather falls more slowly than the stone.
