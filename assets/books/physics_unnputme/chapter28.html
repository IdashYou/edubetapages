Light Waves

Definition of Light Waves

You are surrounded by electromagnetic waves. They're everywhere! From the light you can see, to the infrared your body is producing, to the ultraviolet coming through your window from the sun. You could not escape the waves if you tried. But then again, why would you want to?

The term light waves can be used differently by different people. Physicists tend to casually use 'light waves' to mean exactly the same thing as electromagnetic waves, but most non-physicists do not. So, what is the difference?

Electromagnetic waves (or electromagnetic radiation) are waves made of oscillating magnetic and electric fields, and include radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, x-rays and gamma rays. Like all waves, they carry energy, and that energy can be very high-intensity (like the electromagnetic waves we receive from the sun). When looking at the visible light spectrum, the blue end of the electromagnetic spectrum is high frequency, high energy and short wavelength. The red end of the electromagnetic spectrum is low frequency, low energy and long wavelength.

Light is just one part of the electromagnetic spectrum, the part that our eyes can see. So when most people talk about light waves, this is what they mean. However, in physics, 'light waves' often refer to any and all waves in the electromagnetic spectrum, and this is what we will discuss in this lesson.

Dispersion is due to the fact that the different colours of white light travel at different speeds through the glass and therefore, each colour is refracted at a slightly different direction. Red light is deviated least and therefore travels in glass with the greatest speed while violet is the most deviated with the least speed through glass.

APPLICATION OF LIGHT WAVES 

In a convex mirror the image formed is always virtual, erect and diminished irrespective of the position of the object. For concave mirrors the nature of the image depends on the position, when an object is placed at the focus, the image formed is at infinity. But when an object is placed between the focus and the pole of the mirror the nature of the image formed is virtual, erect and magnified. 

An enlarged and erect image is produced with a concave mirror when the object is positioned between the principal focus and the pole.

In long-sightedness (hyperopia), parallel rays of light are focused behind the retina, so that the person cannot see near object but can see far objects (rays of light from far objects fall on the retina), this is corrected with convex lens. It is caused by eyeball being too short or the lens power being low.

In short-sightedness (myopia), parallel rays of light are focused in front or before the retina, so the person can only see near objects (rays of light from a near object falls on the retina) and cannot see far objects. It is corrected with concave lens. It is caused by a long eyeball or strong lens power.

Ciliary muscles are responsible for power of accommodation in eyes. accommodation of eyes is nothing but the ability of the eye to change its focal length to concentrate on many things. ciliary muscles which control the largening and shortening of lens, help for it.

The iris controls the amount of light by limiting the volume of light admitted. The lens then focuses the light onto the retina, forming an inverted image. The lens changes its shape to adjust the focus on both distant and near objects, a process called accommodation.

Astigmatism is a refractive condition in which the eye is unable to form a point image and is corrected with cylindrical lenses. Myopia and hyperopia are corrected with spherical lenses (concave and convex lens respectively) while presbyopia which is associated with old age is corrected with a convex lens which is also a spherical lens
