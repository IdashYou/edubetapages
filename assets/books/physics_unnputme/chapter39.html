CURRENT AND ELECTRICITY

Electromagnetic induction is the production of electric current or voltage in a conductor whenever their is a relative motion between the conductor and magnetic field.

The two laws of electromagnetic induction are Faraday’s law and Lenz’s law.

Faraday’s law states that whenever there is a change in the magnetic line of force is induced, the strength of which is proportional to the rate of change of the flux linked with the circuit. 

Lenz’s law states that the induced e.m.f is in the direction as to oppose the motion producing it.

The principle of operation of the induction coil was given in 1831 by Michael Faraday. 

Ampere’s law states that two parallel wires attract each other when the current in them flow in the same direction, but repel each other when the currents in them flow in the same direction, but repel each other when the currents in them flow in opposite direction. 

Energy loss in the form of heating effect of the cable can be reduced by making the electricity generated in power stations to be of very high voltage for transmission. A high transmission voltage means only a relatively small current flows through the transmission cables. 

The primary aim in high tension transmission is to minimize electrical energy loses due to heat production. This is achieved by transmitting electricity under high voltage and lower current.

Eddy current is an induced current that damps and generate heat in material leading to energy wastage in most electrical devices. It is only desirable in moving coil instrument and follows Lenz’s law in direction.

AC CIRCUIT

Phasors and Alternating Current

There are two kinds of current. There is the steady constant current called direct current which is the type that batteries supply. The other kind of current is called alternating current which is our main concern in this module. "Alternating" because it's not a steady current. By not steady, we mean it reverses back and forth, flowing in one direction and in the next minute the opposite direction. 

What you may not have known is that most of our devices including TV and mobile phones use alternating current than direct current. And there is a reason for that. AC current is more pliable than DC. You can step up or step-down AC but not DC. Infact, so many characteristics of AC make it inherently better than DC.

It helps to think of DC as a constant that wouldn't change, which is actually what it is. As for AC, we can always use it to our advantage the way we want it. Hence, AC is more widely used in electronics and different kinds of circuit. 

Our focus in this discourse however, would be on how we can solve problems on AC circuit. It's important to understand that the same principles we apply while solving DC circuits is the same principles we need here. All you need is to follow carefully to understand how it works.

Before we go into the problems, we will review the basic concepts that we need here.

A sinusoidal voltage/current might be described by the function

v = Vcos(wt+z)-----(1)    

i = I cos(wt+z)  -----(2)

where v/i is the instantaneous voltage/current, V/I is the maximum/peak voltage and w is the angular frequency and z is the phase angle of the oscillating quantity. 

 

The root mean square value, rms of AC current is the square root of the average of the square of instantaneous current over any given cycle. It turns out we can write an expression relating this value to the peak value of current. A similar expression relates the rms voltage and the peak voltage namely:

Irms = I/√2-----(3)

Vrms = V/√2---(4)

 

The average power into a AC circuit is given by the general expression

Pavg = ½ VI cosz = VrmsIrmscosz-----(5)

 

AC circuit Elements

NB: In an AC circuit that contains only a capacitor, the voltage lags behind the current by 90°

The phase relationship in a purely capacitive circuit the voltage “LAGS” the current by 90°. But by using the voltage as our reference, we can also say that the current “LEADS” the voltage by one quarter of a cycle or 90° as shown in the vector diagram below.

In a Pure inductive Circuit, the current lags behind the voltage by an angle of 90 degrees. While in a Pure Resistive AC circuit, the voltage and current are in phase

The back emf induced in a coil when a current carrying coil is mounted on a metal frame is called eddy current. They are currents induced in conductors, when the conductors are exposed to changing magnetic field due to relative motion of the field source and the conductor.

A simple dc generator consists of a coil of wire rotating in a magnetic field. However, it uses a split ring commutator rather than the two slip rings found in alternating current (ac) generators. To convert an ac dynamo to dc dynamo, the slip rings are replaced with a split-ring commutator.

 

Resistor

The voltage and current through a resistor and in fact every circuit element in an AC circuit is given by eqns (1) and (2) as written above. 

Additionally, we have the relationship for the peak voltage/current through the resistor connected to a AC circuit,

 

V = IR 

 

For a resistor in AC circuit, both the current and voltage are in phase with each other. This means that both current and voltage peak and zero together. When the ac voltage peaks, the lamp (resistor) in the circuit is brightest and vice versa.

 

Inductor

Although there is no resistance, there is a pd that appears across the inductor terminals. Since current varies with time, there is self-induced emf across the inductor terminals by Faraday's law. This induced emf is the source of voltage that appears across the inductor.

By Lenz's law, this emf opposes further current change, making it more and more difficult for the ac current to keep growing/decaying. Hence, the inductor delays current change. After some time, usually on the order of milliseconds, the current finally levels off at a final peak value given by,

V = IXL   XL= wL

XL is called the inductive reactance/resistance,

L is the inductance of the inductor,

I is the peak final/steady current.

 

Eventually, the current lags voltage by 90° in an inductor otherwise spoken, the voltage leads current by 90° in the inductor circuit. This implies that while the voltage across the inductor is highest, the current is minimum and vice versa.

XL = 2 x 30 x 10-3 x 13000 = 245

XL = 245Ω

 

Capacitor

The capacitor opposes voltage change in AC circuit. As more and more charge is deposited on the capacitor plates, it becomes increasingly difficult to keep adding more charge on the plates. 

Hence, voltage change is delayed until the voltage finally levels off at a final steady value. This final steady value of voltage, also called the peak value of voltage is given by,

V = IXC    XC= 1/wC

XC is called the capacitive reactance/resistance,

C is the capacitance of the capacitor,

w=2πf. 

PS: The relationship b/w the peak voltage, current and reactance for each element can all be directly substituted for by the rms values. This means we can replace V and I simultaneously with Vrms and Irms and the equations would remain valid.
