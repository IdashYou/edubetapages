EQUILIBRIUM

A chemical reaction is said to be in equilibrium under three condition 

  1. if the rate of forward reaction is equal to the rate of reverse/backward reaction.
  2. if the change in free energy is zero G = 0
  3. if the change in heat H = TS

where S means change in entropy, and T = temperature. entropy is represented by the capital letter S, and it is a thermodynamic function that describes the randomness and disorder of molecules based on the number of different arrangements available to them in each system or reaction.

Equilibrium constant is defined as the ratio of the rate of forward reaction to that of backward reaction. In writing equilibrium constant for forward reactions, the products are placed as the numerator while the reactants are placed as the denominator. In writing reverse or backward equilibrium constant, reactants from the numerator while products from the denominator. 

Forward equilibrium constant = KC

Reverse equilibrium constant = KCl

If the chemical reaction is multiplied by a factor n, the new equilibrium constant will be raised to the nth power.

If the equilibrium constant is equal to one (k = 1), then the reaction is at equilibrium.

If the equilibrium constant is greater than one (k > 1), the forward reaction is favored, but if the equilibrium constant is less than one (k < 1), then the backward reaction is favored.

In a reversible reaction Initially in a reversible reaction the rate stabilizes with time, the rate of forward reaction is usually faster than the backward reaction. After a certain time, the rate of backward reaction becomes equal, leading to Equilibrium. 

During equilibrium calculation solids and liquids are constant and thus neglected.

Equilibrium constant is affected by enthalpy and it is only temperature change that can affect enthalpy change. This means that concentration change, change in pressure, and even application of catalyst, have no effect on it.

Enthalpy change is the name given to the amount of heat evolved or absorbed in a reaction carried out at constant pressure. It is given the symbol ΔH, read as "delta H". Note: The term "enthalpy change" only applies to reactions done at constant pressure.

If the number of moles of gaseous reactants becomes equal to the number of moles of gaseous product, then pressure or volume has no effect.

In equilibrium condition an Increase in pressure favours the side of the reaction with lesser number of moles while decrease in pressure favours the side with smaller number of moles.

(2) When at equilibrium, which of the reactions below will shift to the right if the pressure is increased and the temperature is kept constant? 

A. 2SO3(g)<—>2SO2(g) + O2(g)

B. 2CO2(g)<—>2CO(g) + O2(g)

C. 2H2(g) + O2(g)<—>2H2O(g)

D. 2NO(g) <—> N2(g) + O2(g)

Option C 2015

Explanation

This is an equilibrium (reversible) reaction. The effects of temperature, pressure/number of moles and concentration on the equilibrium position of a reversible reaction are summarized below:

Effect of Temperature

(a) Given A + B <—> C + D   ∆H = +ve 

In the above reaction, the forward reaction is endothermic while the backward reaction is exothermic. Increasing the temperature of the system will shift the equilibrium to the right, thereby favouring the forward reaction. This means more products (C and D) will be formed. 

Reducing the temperature favours the backward reaction ie more reactants would be produced.

If the forward reaction were rather exothermic, the reaction will proceed in opposite direction as described above. Increasing the temperature would favour the left side (endothermic side) and reducing it will then favour the forward (exothermic side).

Effect of Pressure

(b) Say the system 2A(g) + 3B <—> C(g) + 3D(g) is in equilibrium

Before pressure can affect a equilibrium reaction, the number of moles gaseous reactants on the left would be different from the number on the product side. 

It also follows without saying that at least one of the reactants or products would have to be a gas if pressure will affect the equilibrium of the system.

In the reaction above, notice that the left side has 2 moles of gas while the product side has 4 moles total of gas. 

Therefore, if the pressure of the system is increased, the side with less number of moles will be favoured which in this case is the reactant side. A decrease in pressure will favour the right side with greater number of moles.

Effect of Concentration

(c) If the concentration of reactants is increased, the equilibrium will favour the product side since more reactants are introduced. 

Similarly, removing the products as they are formed leaves the reactants with more concentration, leading to the continuous formation of products.

On the contrary, reducing the concentration of reactants will favour the left side ie reactant formation. 

Back to our question, look at the third reaction 2H2(g) + O2(g) –> 2H2O(g)

From the above discussion, the right side has 2 moles which is less than the 3 gas moles on the left. So, increasing the pressure will shift the equilibrium to the right like we predicted.
