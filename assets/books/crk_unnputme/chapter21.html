Summary of the early life of Joseph and his arrival in Egypt

Joseph was one of the twelve sons of Jacob, and the second son of Racheal, one of the Wives of Jacob. At the young age of seventeen he took up the task of shepherding his father’s flock with his brothers during which he brought an ill report of his brothers to their father. 

Jacob (Israel) loved Joseph more than any other of his children because he was the child of his old age and also made for him a long robe with sleeves. These probably made his brothers conclude that their father loved him more than them and as a result they hated him and could not speak peacefully to him. 

It happened that Joseph had a dream one day where they were binding sheaves in the field and behold his sheaf grew and stood upright to the extent that his brothers sheaves gathered round it and bowed down to his sheaf. 

His brothers were annoyed and said to him, “Are you indeed to reign over us? Or are you indeed to have dominion over us?” So, his brothers hated him the more for his dreams and for his words. Again, Joseph dreamt and told his brothers. In this one, the sun, the moon and eleven stars were bowing down for him. 

But after narrating this to his father and his brothers, his father rebuked him and said to him, “what is this dream that you have dreamed? Shall I and your mother and your brothers indeed come to bow ourselves on the ground before you?” His brothers were however jealous of him, but his father kept the saying in his mind. 

One day his brothers went to pasture their father’s flock near Shechem and Joseph was not with them. So, Jacob sent Joseph from the valley of Hebron to see how his brothers were fairing at Shechem and bring him words. When Joseph got to Shechem a man saw him while he was wandering and asked him what he was seeking, Joseph replied that he was seeking his brothers and further asked the man whether he saw them. The man then directed him to go to Dothan. So, Joseph went after his brothers and found them at Dothan. 

When they saw him from afar, they conspired to kill him, saying to one another, “Here comes this dreamer. Come now, let us kill him and throw him into one of the pits; then we shall say that a wide beast has devoured him, and we shall see what will come of his dreams.

Reuben was however at hand to deliver him out of the hands of his cruel brothers saying, “let us not take his life.” He further said to them, “Shed no blood; cast him into this pit here in the wilderness, but lay no hand upon him. He said this so that he will rescue him from them and restore him to his father. 

When Joseph came to his brothers, they removed his robe which was the robe with sleeves which their father made for him. They took him and cast him into an empty pit that has no water. 

Then they sat to eat and on looking up they saw a caravan of Ishmaelite coming from Gilead with their camels bearing gum, balm, and myrrh going down to Egypt. Judah one of Joseph’s brothers said to them, “What profit is it if we slay our brother and conceal his blood? Come, let us sell him to the Ishmaelite’s, and let not our hand be upon him, for he is our brother, our own flesh. "This advice was accepted by his brothers who drew Joseph up and lifted him from pit and then sold him to the Ishmaelites for twenty shekels of silver. The Ishmaelites took him down to Egypt where they later re-sold him to Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh.

Summary of Joseph as the governor of Egypt

After two years, while Joseph was in the prison, pharaoh had a dream in which he was standing by the Nile and there came out of the Nile seven cows sleek and fat feeding on the reed grass. And behold, seven other cows, gaunt and thin, came up out of the same Nile and stood by the other cows by the bank of the Nile. And the thin and the gaunt cows ate up the seven sleek and fat cows and still remained thin that no one would know that they had eaten them. 

Again, Pharaoh had another dream where he saw seven ears of grain, plump and good, that were growing on one stalk. Then another seven ears of the same grain sprouted out which were thin and blighted by the east wind. The thin ears swallowed the seven plump and full ears. 

The dreams troubled Pharaoh that he summoned all the magicians from Egypt and all its wise men and told them the dream he had, but none of the could interpret be it to him. It was then his chief butler (servant) who told him how he met a young Hebrew in custody when he offended the Pharaoh, who was a servant of the captain of the guard and how the young Hebrew servant interpreted their dreams to them and it came to pass. Immediately pharaoh sent for Joseph and he was brought out of the dungeon. 

After shaving and changing his clothes he was brought before the king and the king told him the dream which no one could interpret. Joseph said to him, “It is not in me; God will give pharaoh a favourable answer.” Having narrated the dreams to Joseph he said to Pharaoh, “The dream of Pharaoh is one; God has revealed to Pharaoh what he is about to do.” He explained that the seven good cows are seven years, and the seven good ears are seven years; the dream is one. 

The seven lean and gaunt cows that came up after them are seven years, and the seven empty ears blighted by the east wind are also seven years of famine. He further maintained that there will come seven years of great plenty throughout the land of Egypt, which will be followed by another seven years of famine. 

The famine will severe and consummate the land and the period of plenty will be forgotten in Egypt. He stressed that the doubling of the dream indicated that it was fixed by God and that God will shortly bring it to pass. 

On the remedy to avert the great famine Joseph advised Pharaoh to select a man who is discreet and wise and set him over the land of Egypt. He also advised him to appoint overseers over the land to take the fifth part of the produce of the land of Egypt during the seven plenteous years. And to gather all the food of these good years that are Coming and keep them in stock as reserve for the land against the seven years of famine which are to befall the land of Egypt. 

The proposal was good to pharaoh and his servants that he said to them, “Can we find such a man as this, in whom is the Spirit of God?” So pharaoh said to Joseph, “Since God has shown you all this, there is none so discreet and wise as you are; you shall be over my house, all my people shall order themselves as you command; only as regards the throne shall I be greater than you.” Pharaoh set him over all the land of Egypt, gave him his signet ring and arrayed him in garment of fine clothes with gold chain about his neck. Joseph was also given the second chariot of the king which by implication made him the second in command after Pharaoh. 

Moreover, Pharaoh said to Joseph, “I am Pharaoh, and without your consent no man shall lift up his hand or foot in all the land of Egypt." As a mark of his new office, Pharaoh gave him the name, Zaphenath-paneah,” and also gave him Asenath the daughter of Portiphera the priest of On in mage. Joseph was thirty years old when he entered the service of Pharaoh, the king of Egypt. 

During the seven years of plenteous harvest, Joseph was up to his task by gathering food and storing them up in every city from the fields around them. He stored food in great abundance like the sand of the sea, until he ceased to measure it, for it could not be measured. 

Before the year of famine, Joseph had two sons namely Manaseh, which means “God has made me forget all my hardship and all my father’s house” and Ephraim, meaning “For God has made me fruitful in the land of my affliction.” The seven years of plenty came to an end and the years of famine began just as Joseph said. 

There was famine in all the lands except in Egypt. When the whole land of Egypt was famished, they cried to Pharaoh for bread who sent them to Joseph with the order, “Go to Joseph; what he says to you, do.” So, when the famine spread all over the land, Joseph Opened all the storehouses, and sold to the Egyptians for the famine was severe and even people from all over the earth do go to Egypt for bread.

Summary of Gen. 45:1-15

The famine that broke out affected the earth greatly that Egypt became a pilgrimage centre of its sort. The whole nations of the earth go the Egypt to buy their food. The children of Jacob were among the people who come to Egypt to buy food. 

In one of their trips when Joseph could not control himself again, he ordered all the people who were with him away and made himself known to his brothers who did not recognize him again and cried bitterly. 

When he asked them whether their father was still alive, they could not stand his presence probably for self guilt and this made them dismayed before Joseph. In order to clear their guilt, he made them to understand that it was God and not them who made him to come to Egypt for the preservation of life. 

Having made them understand his divine mission, he instructed his brothers to go back and bring his father and their entire household to Egypt where they shall live in the land of Goshen. Then he fell upon his brother Benjamin, wept and kissed all his brothers as a sign of love and true reconciliation and his brothers talked with him.
