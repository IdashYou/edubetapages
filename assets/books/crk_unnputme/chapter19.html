Summary of the order of creation in Genesis 1 

The Genesis 1 account gave the prelude that in the beginning, God created the heaven and the earth. At that point, the earth was without form and void, and darkness was upon the face of the deep. and the spirit of God was moving over the face of the waters. The creation of the world took God six days while he rested on the seventh day. The order is as follows: 

First day: Light was created and was separated from the darkness to form day and night. 

Second day: The heaven or the firmament was created. 

Third day: Land, sea and vegetations were created. 

Fourth day: The heavenly bodies or the luminaries (sun, moon and stars). 

Fifth day: Sea animals and birds. 

Sixth day: Land animals and man were created.

In the creation of man, special attention was giving to him When God said, “let us make man in our image and likeness; and let them have dominion over the fish of the sea, and over the birds of the air, and over the cattle, and over all the earth, and over every creeping thing that creeps upon the earth” (Gen. 1:26). 

God created them male and female with the blessing, “Be fruitful and multiply, and fill the earth and subdue it...” (v.28). 

Finally, having completed his work of creation and seeing that they were all good, God rested on the seventh day. He also blessed the day and hallowed it because on it he rested from all his work which he had done on creation.

Summary of the order of creation in Genesis 2 

The creation account in Genesis 2 centered on the creation of man which unlike in Genesis 1 where man was created out of nothing, Genesis 2 records that God created man out of the dust. [n the creation of man in Genesis 2, the Lord God formed man of dust from the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life and upon that man became a living being. Thereafter God planted a garden in Eden, in the east where he put the man he created. God made so many good trees that are good for food to grow and in the midst of the garden was the tree of life and the of the knowledge of good and evil. There was a river that flowed out of Eden to water the garden and there it divided and became four rivers. 

These are Pishon, Gihon, Tigris and Euphrates. Pishon flows around the whole land of Havilah; Gihon flows around the whole land of Cush; while Tigris flows east of Assyria. God placed man in the garden to till and keep it. 

He also commanded him to eat every tree of the garden but to stay away from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil warning that any time he eats of it he (man) will die. God then saw that it was not good that the man should be alone; he therefore decided to make a helpmate for him. 

Out of the ground the Lord made all the beast of the field and the birds of the air, these he brought to the man to see what he will call them. The man gave name to all cattle, birds and to every beast of the field, but no helpmate was found suitable for the man. 

As a result, God caused a deep sleep to fall upon the man and while he was asleep, God took one of the man’s ribs and enclosed it with flesh. He built the rib into a woman and brought her to the man who exclaimed, “This at last is bones and ‘flesh of my flesh; she shall be called woman, because she was taken Out of man” (Gen. 2; 23), This explains why a man should leave his father and his mother and Cleaves to his wife, and they become one flesh. 

At that point the man and the woman were both naked, and were not ashamed.

To the Choirmaster. A Psalm of David.

[2] Day to day pours forth speech, and night to night declares knowledge.

[3] There is no speech, nor are there words; their voice is not heard;

[4] yet their voice goes out through all the earth, and their words to the end of the world. In them he has set a tent for the sun,

[5] which comes forth like a bridegroom leaving his chamber, and like a strong man runs its course with joy.

[6] Its rising is from the end of the heavens, and its circuit to the end of them; and there is nothing hid from its heat.

Summary of Psalms Account

The account in Psalm 19 is an allude to the creative work of God, his glory and a continuation of his power. In verse I, the heavens affirm the glory of God and the firmament proclaims the work of his hands. By implication, the heavenly hosts are attesting to the supremacy of God. 

From day to day, and night to night, the power and glory of God are manifested though these are not expressed in concrete words or speeches, yet their voice goes out through all the earth and their words to the end of the world. 

In them God set a tent for the sun, which comes forth like a bridegroom leaving his chamber, and like a strong man, runs its course with joy. 

Its rising is from the end of the heavens, and its circuit to the end of them; and there is nothing hid from its heat.
