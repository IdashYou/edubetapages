Summary of Isaiah 45:9-12

Prophet Isaiah likens the nature of God to an earthen vessel and the potter. The relationship is such that exists between a master, who has absolute control and the servant who is a subject to the master and has no power of its own. Isaiah therefore maintained that the clay as a subject cannot challenge its maker or the potter. 

He said, “Woe to him who strives with his Maker, an earthen vessel with the potter.” He went further to insist that the clay does not question the one who fashions it and woes anyone who says to the father, “What are you begetting? or to a woman, with what are you in travail?” These are impossibilities and clear expression of the masters’ superiority over their subjects. 

In verses 11 and 12, the sovereign nature of God is affirmed as follows: 

“Will you question me about my children, or command me concerning the work of my hands? made the earth, and created man upon it; it was my hands that stretched out the heavens, and I commanded all their host.”

Summary of Daniel 4: 4-37

In the book of Daniel, King Nebuchadnezzar is king over one of the powerful empire that has ever existed, the Babylon Empire. He was arrogant, confident, and it appeared that he was in absolute control of everything within the Empire and that he was sovereign over his own life and kingdom. 

However, in the fourth Chapter of Daniel he had a dream. Which disturbed him greatly that he summoned his magicians, the enchanters, the Chaldeans, the astrologers and sorcerers to interpret to him what the dream meant, but they could not help him. 

At last Daniel who was named Belteshazzar was called and asked to interpret the dream after the king had narrated the dream to him. Not being comfortable with the dream prophet Daniel was alarmed and said, “My lord, may the dream be for those who hate you and its interpretation for your enemies! 

The tree you saw, which grew and became strong, so that its top reached to heaven, and it was visible to the end of the whole earth; whose leaves were fair and its fruit abundant, and in which was food for all; under which beasts of the field found shade, and in whose branches the birds of the air dwelt - it's you, O king, who have grown and become strong” (4: 20-22). 

The interpretation of the dream suggested that the king has become power drunk and sees himself as a sovereign being. As a result, prophet Daniel pointed out that he will be driven away from the people and made to live with the wild animals, where he will be made to eat grass like an oxen until he acknowledges that the Most High is sovereign and rules over the kingdoms of men. 

He however advised the king to accept his counsel by leaving his sins and practicing righteousness through showing mercy to the oppressed.

Fulfillment of the Dream and the King’s Confession

At the end of twelve months, while the king was walking on the roof of the royal palace of Babylon, he said, “ls not this great Babylon, which I have built with my mighty power as a royal residence and for the glory of my majesty?” 

While the words were still in his mouth, there fell a voice from heaven, “O king Nebuchadnezzar, to you it is spoken: The kingdom has departed from you, and you shall be driven from among men, and your dwelling shall be with the beasts of the field; and you shall be made to eat grass like an oxen; and seven times shall pass over you, until you have learned that the Most High rules the kingdom of men and gives it to whom he will” (Daniel 4: 31-32). 

Immediately the king was driven from among men and he ate grass like an ox, and his body was drenched with the dew of heaven till his hair grew as long as eagles’ feathers and his nails were like birds’ claws. 

At last when the king regained his reasoning, he acknowledged the Sovereignty of God and his everlasting dominion. Having realized this, he blessed, praised and honoured God who lives for ever and whose kingdom endures from generation to generation. His confession (Daniel 4:28-37) is that of a man humbled before the source of absolute power and authority.
