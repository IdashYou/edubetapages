KINGDOM CLASSIFICATION

Classification is an act of grouping things together on the basis of features they have in cannon. The science of classification is called Taxonomy.
TAXONOMY
The science of taxonomy has two branches;

  1. The naming of organisms otherwise known as Nomenclature
  2. The placing of organism into group or Systematic.

The latter is done on the basis of their similarities and difference.
Biological nomenclature is based on the binomial system pioneered by the work of the Swedish naturalist Carl Linnaeus (1707-78).

In this system, each organism has two Latin name: a generic name beginning with a letter and a specific name beginning with a lower case letter. For example, humans are named Homo sapiens; the genus is Homo and the species is sapiens. Italics are used to indicate Latin names. Alternatively, the words can be underlined e.g Homo sapiens

Species: Can be defined as a group of closely related organisms which are capable of interbreeding to produce fertile offspring occasionally, two organisms which are genetically closely related can interbred to produce infertile offspring. A cross (hybrid) between a donkey and a horse, produce a male, which is in fertile.
There are two types of classification;

  1. Artificial
  2. Natural

An artificial classification is based on one or a few easily observed characteristics.
A natural classification tries to use natural relationship between organisms. It considers more evidence than artificial classifications including internal as well as external features.
Similarities of embryology, morphology, anatomy, physiology, biochemistry, cell structure and behavior are all relevant. Most classification in use today are natural and phylogenetic.
A phylogenetic classification is one based on evolutionary relationships. Another type is phonetic classification. This avoids the problem of establishing evolutionary relationships, which can be very difficult and very controversial.
The use Of computers in taxonomy is known as numerical taxonomy.

FIVE KINGDOMS OF CLASSIFICATION
The five-kingdom scheme was proposed by Whittaker (1969). Whittaker's scheme consists of the:

  1. kingdom – Monera
  2. kingdom - Protista
  3. kingdom – fungi
  4. kingdom – plantae
  5. kingdom – animalia

N.B: the viruses do not fit into any of the five kingdoms because they are not seen as true living things.

(i) KINGDOM MONERA
Organisms in this kingdom are very tiny and simple in-form. They are the simplest micro-organisms. The body consist of one cell, the cell lacks a well organized nucleus- no nuclear membrane, the nuclear materials just lie free in the cell (hence monerans are called "prokaryotes". The cell also lacks mitochondria and plastid. However, it is enclosed by a rigid cell wall. Reproduction is asexual by binary fission or budding. Examples of monerans are the (a) bacteria and (b) blue-green algae.

The bacteria are unicellular organisms of the phylum schizomycophyta. They show various shapes. Some are autotrophic, others are saprophytic and parasitic. Some bacteria are aerobic while others are anaerobic. They reproduce by binary fission. The difference between prokaryotes and eukaryotes are shown below.

Prokaryotes
The prokaryote is made up of organisms commonly known as Bacteria. They are mainly single cells.

Features of prokaryotes

  1. Cell wall: The bacterial cell wall is strong and rigid due to the present of murein, a molecule that consists of parallel polysaccharides cross-linked at regular intervals by short chain of amino-acids.
  2. Cell membrane: the structure and functions of the cell surface membrane are similar to those in eukaryotic cells. It is also the site of some respiratory enzymes. In some bacteria, it forms mesosomes and/or photosynthetic membranes. Mesosomes are folding of the cell surface membrane. They appear to be associated with DNA during cell division.
  3. Genetic materials: DNA is circular and lies free in the cytoplasm (no true nucleus).
  4. Ribosome: is the site of protein synthesis.
  5. Capsules: capsules are slimy or gummy secretions of certain bacteria which show up clearly after negatives staining. In some cases, these secretions unite bacteria into colonies. They also enable bacteria to stick to surface such as teeth, mud and rocks.
  6. Spores: Some bacteria, mainly of the genera Clostridium and Bacillus form endospores (spore produced inside cells). They are thick-walled, long-lived and extremely resistant particularly to heat, drought and short-wave radiations.
  7. Flagella: Flagella of bacteria are simple, lacking microtubules. It is 20nm in diameter. It is rigid and well shaped. It is used for movement. Examples of bacteria with flagella are Rhizobium (one flagellum), Azotobacter (many flagella) both of which are involved in the nitrogen cycle;
  8.  Pili (singular pilus): Projecting from the wall of some Gram negative bacteria are numerous fine protein rods called Pili or fimbriae. They are shorter and thinner than flagella and are concerned with attachment to specific cells or surfaces. Various types occur, but of particular interest is the F. Pilus. This involved in sexual reproduction.
  9. Cell shape: bacterial shape is important aid to classification.

Form of Bacteria
1. Cocci:
(a)Streptococci (chain) e.g. Streptococcus pneumonia
(b) Staphylococci (like a Bunch of grapes) e.g. Staphylococcus aureus.
2. Bacilli: (single bacillus): rod-shaped e.g. Escherichia coli, Salmonella typhi (causes typhoid fever).
3. Spirilla (singular: spirillum) spiral- shaped e.g. Treponema palladium causes syphilis.

VIRUSES
Viruses were discovered in 1852, by a Russian botanist, D. l lvanovskyby preparing an infectious extract from tobacco plants suffering from mosaic disease.
Characteristics of virus

  1. They are the smallest living organisms.
  2. They do not have a cellular structure.
  3. They can only reproduce by invading living cells. Therefore, they are parasitic. They are obligate endoparasites, meaning that they can only live parasitically inside other cell. Outside the living cell, they form crystal and become inactive.
  4. They have a simple structure consisting of a small piece of nucleic acid either DNA or RNA, not both, surrounded by a protein or lipoprotein coat.
  5. They are the boundary between what we regard as living and non-living.
  6. Each type of virus recognizes and infects only certain type of cell. I.e. virus is highly specific to their hosts.

Structure of virus
Viruses have a very simple structure consisting of the following:
(i). Core: The genetic material, either DNA or RNA. The DNA or RNA may be single-stranded or double -stranded.
(ii)  Capsid: a protective coat of protein surrounding the core.

  1. Nucleocapsid: the combined structure formed by the core and capsid.
  2. Envelope: A few viruses such as the HIV and influenza viruses have an additional lipoprotein layer around the capsid derived from the cell surface membrane of the host cell.
  3. Capsomeres: capsids are often built up of identical repeating subunits called capsomeres.

Viruses that attack bacteria form a group called bacteriophages.
 
HIV
AIDS (Acquired Immune deficiency Syndrome) is caused by HIV (Human
Immunodeficiency Virus). HIV belongs to a group of RNA viruses known as Retro viruses. This name comes from the fact that these viruses can convert their RNA back into a DNA copy using an enzyme known as reverse Transcriptase. Normally a section of DNA (gene) is copied to make RNA, a process called transcription. Making DNA from RNA is therefore reverse transcription, and the enzyme controlling it is called Reverse transcriptase.

Kingdom Protista
Member of this kingdom are unicellular organisms but much larger than the monerans. They are eukaryotes i.e. their nucleus is enclosed in a nuclear membrane. Examples of Protista include Algae, Protozoa, Oomycota and slime moulds. Better illustration is shown below.
(i) Algae: The algae form a large group of Protista of great biological importance and significance to humans. The bodies of algae lack true stems. roots and leaves. Such relatively undifferentiated body is called a thallus. Two examples of algae are namely chlorella (phylum chlorophyta) and fucus (Phylum Phaeophyta).
(A) Phylum Chlorophyta (green algae).
Chlorella is a unicellular non-motile green alga. In phylum chlorophyta (green algae), the dominant photosynthetic pigment is chlorophyll; therefore green in appearance. Chlorophyll is present (as in plants). They store carbohydrate as starch (insoluble). They are mostly fresh water organism.
Example of chlorophyta includes chorella, chlorophyta, chlamydomonas, spirogyra, ulva (e.t.c)
(B) Phylum Phaeophyta (brown algae)
Fucus is a relatively large and complex brown alga. In phaeophyta, dominant photosynthetic pigment is brown and called fucoxanthin. They store carbohydrates as soluble Linamarin
and Mannitol. They also store fat. Nearly all marine (fresh water genera only).

Morphological adaptation of brown algae. The thallus is firmly anchored by a holdfast. This form an intimate association with its substrate, usually rock, and is extremely difficult to dislodge.

Physiological adaptation: The dominant photosynthetic pigment is the brown pigment fucoxanthin. This is an adaptation for photosynthesizing under water because fucoxanthin strongly absorbs blue light which penetrates water much further than longer wavelengths such as red light.

Reproductive adaptation: Release of gametes is synchronized with the tides. At low tide the thallus dries and squeezes the sex organs, which are protected by mucilage out of the conceptacles.

(2) PROTOZOA
Protozoa are unicellular, animal- like protista with heterotrophic nutrition. Most are free- living and they have various methods of locomotion. Some, however, are parasites, including one (plasmodium) which causes malaria. Examples of protozoa include amoeba, paramecium, Plasmodium, etc.

PHYLUM PROTOZOA
They are microscopic, unicellular organisms. Members can be free living, mutualistic or Parasitic. There are four main classes. The classification is based on locomotory structures,

  1. Class Rhizopoda or Sarcodina e.g.(Amoeba, Difflugia).These are the most primitive protozoans. Members have pseudopodia for movement and feeding. Asexual reproduction is by binary fission.
  2. Class mastigophora or flagellate (e.g. Euglena, trypanosome. Noctiluca, volvox, chlamydomonas). Members possess one or more flagella for movement at the adult stage. The body is covered by a firm pellicle which gives the body a definite shape.
  3. Class ciliophora or ciliata. (e.g. paramecium, opalina, balantidium). Members possesses cilia as locomotory organs. There are usually two types of nuclei the meganucleus and micronucleus. Sexual reproduction is by conjugation.
  4. Class sporozoa (e.g. plasmodium, Eimeria, monocysts). Members lack pellicle, contractile vacuoles and locomotory organelles. They reproduce sexually or asexually.

 

Phylum cilliophora (ciliates)
Ciliate are type of protozoan. They have the following characteristic:

  1.      They are unicellular, heterotrophic organisms.
  2. They posses Cilia, fine hairs which beat and cause movement of water, either for locomotion or feeding.
  3. They are definite shape due to the presence of a thin, flexible outer region of cytoplasm called the pellicle.
  4. They have a complex cell structure with a macronucleus and a micronucleus.

Example of ciliate is paramecium. It lives in stagnant water, or slow-flowing fresh water that contains decay organic matter. Other example of ciliates are Opalina and Balantidium. They have two fixed contractile vacuoles present in the endoplasm. They are responsible for osmo-regulation. Their cells contain two nuclei. The larger, bean-shape macronucleus is polyploid (has more than two sets of chromosomes). It controls metabolic activities apart from reproduction. The micronucleus is diploid. It controls reproduction and the formation of new macronuclei during nuclear division. Paramecium can reproduce both asexually (by transverse binary fission) and sexually (by conjugation).

Phylum apicomplexa
This group of protozoan also possesses a pellicle, giving the cell a definite shape. Most however, possess no special shape for locomotion and have limited movement. Their most distinguishing characteristic is the production of spores during asexual and sexual reproduction. An example is the parasitic plasmodium which causes malaria in humans.

Kingdom Fungi
The fungi are a large and successful group of organisms of about 80,000 named species. The range in size from the unicellular yeasts to the large toadstools, puffballs and stinkhorns, and occupy a very wide range of habitats, both aquatic and terrestrial.

Characteristics of fungi
They exhibit heterotrophic nutrition because they lack chlorophyll and are therefore non-photosynthetic. They can be parasites, saprotrophs or . mutualists. Nutrition is absorptive; digestion takes place outside the body and nutrients are absorbed directly. Digestion does not take place inside the body, unlike animal. They have rigid cell walls that contain chitin. Body is usually a mycelium, a network of fine tubular filaments called Hyphae these may be septate (have cross walls) e.g. penicillium or Aseptate (no cross wall) e.g. mucor. If carbohydrate is stored, it is usually as glycogen, not starch. Reproduce by mean of spores. They are non-motile.

Classification of fungi

  1. Phylum ZygomycotA
  2. Phylum Ascomycota
  3. Phylum Basidiomycota

(4) KINGDOM PLANTAE
Plants are autotrophic eukaryotes which are adapted to grow on land. Plants are photoautotrophic; meaning their source of energy is light. This method of nutrition is called photosynthesis. Generally, plants can be grouped into;

  1.  Non-vascular plants
  2.  Vascular plants.
  3. Non-vascular plants: are plants that lack vascular bundles- xylem and phloem e.g. bryophytes
  4. Vascular plants: Are plant that have vascular bundles. Example includes filicinophyta (Ferns) coniferophyta (conifers), angiospermophyta (flowering plants).

Division bryophyte (liverworts and mosses)
Bryophytes are multicellular, non-flowering and non-vascular plants. They were probably the first land plants that were not totally dependent on the aquatic media for survival. Bryophytes have no true roots, stems and leaves but have structure resembling these and so are more advanced than the algae and fungi. Bryophytes show a clearly defined alternation of generation. The sporophyte is always attached and dependent on the gametophyte.
They are thought to have evolved from green algae. The division contains two main classes: the hepaticae or liverworts and mosses.
Bryophytes have no true vascular tissue (xylem or phloem). They lack true root, being anchored by thin filaments called Rhizoids which grow from the stem. They exhibit alternation of generations, in which the gametophyte generation is dominant.

Division Fllicinophyta (Ferns)
The pteridophytes (filicinophytes) or the ferns are non flowering vascular plants. They exhibit alternation of generations in which the sporophyte is dominant. Gametophyte is reduced to a small, simple prothlllus. Typically, fern leaves are coiled when young and unroll as they grow to form large mature leaves. The stem of the fern has a protective epidermis and supporting vascular tissue (xylem and phloem). Xylem and phloem bare concerned with translocation (transport) of water and nutrients round the plant body. Xylem carries mainly water and mineral salt, whereas phloem carries mainly organic solutes in solution such as sugars. The sporophyte of fern is the persisting plant, commonly recognized as the fern plant. The gametophyte is a heart shaped structure which bears the sex organs in which eggs and male gametes or sperms are produce. Unlike the bryophytes, the sporophytes and gametophytes of ferns lead separate existences. Some common examples of ferns are Dryopteris, Adiantum (maidenhair fern) and Nephrolepsis.

THE SPERMATOPHYTA
The spermatophytes are the seed-producing vascular plants, having well developed roots, stems, and leaves. The seeds have structures that enclose the embryo. The sporophyte is the dominant generation, gametophyte generation is severely reduced. The sporophyte produces two types of spores i.e. (heterosporous).The two types are microspores and megaspores. Water is not needed for sexual reproduction because male gametes do not swim.
The spermatophytes are divided into

  1. Gymnosperms
  2. Angiosperms

GYMNOSPERMS
The Gymnosperms are plants with naked seeds. They are non-flowering, seed-producing vascular plants. They have true-roots, stems and leaves. The leaves of most gymnosperms are characteristically tine needle-like structures or tiny scale-like structures. The seeds of these plants are borne in special structure called cones. The gymnosperms are more advance and better adapted for terrestrial life than the ferns. Cycas and Pinus are example of gymnosperms.

ANGIOSPERMOPHYTA (Flowering Plants)
Angiosperms form the largest group in the plant kingdom. The angiosperms are more highly evolved than the gymnosperms because they have an abundance of xylem vessels and bear seeds which are projected within fruits. The angiosperm consists of the dicotyledons and the monocotyledons.

(B) PHYLUM CNIDARIAN (COELENTRATES)
Members are simple multicellular invertebrate animals, mainly Marine.
Characteristics of cnidarians

  1. They are radially symmetrical.
  2. They are diploblastic animals i.e. body wall composed of two layers of cells, an outer ectoderm and an inner endoderm; these layers are separated by a structure-less, gelatinous layer of mesogloea which may contain cells that have migrated from other layers.
  3. They possess a gut with a single opening, the mouth, which is fringing by tentacles. Each tentacles bears stinging cells called cnidoblasts which can paralyses prey. Two different structural types are found in the coelenterates: polyps and medusa.

Polyps are sessile (stay in one place) and may be solitary e.g. hydra, or colonial e.g. Obelia. Medusa are free-swimming and solitary they exhibit polymorphism. Cnidarians are divided into three classes namely:

  1. Class hydrozoa e.g. hydra
  2. Class scyphozoa e.g. Jelly fish
  3. Class Anthozoa e.g. sea anemones, corals.

(E) PHYLUM ARTHROPODA
The phylum arthropoda is the largest phylum in the animal kingdom. An extremely diverse phylum, the arthropoda include the lobsters, crabs, spiders, mites, scorpions, centipedes, millipedes and insect. They are distributed in all habitat.
Characteristics of the arthropoda

  1. they are bilaterally symmetrical
  2. they are triploblastic
  3. they are eucoelomates i.e they have true body cavity
  4. they are metamerically segmented
  5. they have jointed appendages
  6. The body is covered with exoskeleton made up of chitin.
  7. They have a body divided into a head, thorax and abdomen.

Arthropoda is divided into five classes;
(1)Class crustacean: (e.g. crayfish, lobster, prawn, shrimp). Members live in water. They have two pair of antennae. The appendages are many. Their typical gaseous exchange is gills. They have a pair of compound eyes raised on stalks.
 
(2)Class insecta: members are the insects. They live in various habitats; they have three body division in the head, thorax and abdomen. The head bears a pair of compound eyes and a pair of antennae. The thorax bears 2 pairs of wings and 3 pair of legs. They exhibit metamorphosis which could be complete or incomplete.
 
(3) Class chilopoda (centipedes); they are mainly terrestrial. Member dwells in moist environment. The body is divided into numerous similar segments which give a worm-like appearance. No larval form gaseous exchange is by tracheae. They have poisonous claws. They are mainly carnivorous

(4) Class diplopoda (millipedes): they are terrestrial. Defined head with a pair of antennae. Numerous legs, all identical. No larval stage. Gaseous exchange is through the tracheae. They are herbivorous e.g. millipedes.

(5)Class Arachinida: these include the spiders, scorpions, mites and ticks. They have two body division- the cephalo thorax and abdomen. The lack antennae, compound eyes and wings. The cephalo thorax carries four pairs of legs. No larval stage. Gaseous exchange is by 'lung' books or 'gill' books or tracheae

THE VERTEBRATES
Phylum chordata belongs to this group. Animals in this group have the following features:

  1. All have a solid skeletal rod called notochord (or chorda dorsalis).
  2. They have tubular nerve cord. This is dorsally located, above the notochord.

  4. They are bilaterally symmetrical
  5. Their sense organs are highly developed.
  6. They are triploblastic, coelomate animals.
  7. They have pairs of gill slits or visceral clefts.
  8. They have a post anal-tail i.e. a tail after the anus
  9. The heart is ventrally placed
