INTRODUCTION

Biology is a branch of natural science which deals with the study of life (living organisms)

The term Biology is of Greek origin "bios" meaning "life" and "logy" meaning the study of life. Traditionally, the main branches of Biology are Botany  and Zoology.

(a)    Botany: this is the study of plants

(b)    Zoology: this is the study of animals.

There are further divisions under both fields, these include:

i.            ANATONY: This is the study of structure of plants and animals.

ii.            BIOCHEMISTRY: This is the study of the chemistry of living organism. It also includes the substances which the organisms produce.

iii.            CYTOLOGY: This is the detailed study of cells which include their origin and structure. Also called  Cell Biology

iv.            HISTOLOGY: This is the study of tissues and their formation into organs.

v.            PHYSIOLOGY: This is the study of the numerous activities and processes which take place in an organism.

vi.            BACTERIOLOGY: This is the scientific study of bacteria.

vii.            ECOLOGY: this is the study of the relationship between organisms and their habitats.

viii.            ENTOMOLOGY: this is the study of insects.

ix.            GENETICS: this is the study of inheritance.

x.            ICHTHYOLOGY: This is the study of fishes

xi.            MAMMALOGY: this is the study of mammals.

xii.            MICROBIOLOGY: This is the scientific study of micro-organisms.

xiii.            NEMATOLOGY: this is the study of nematodes.

xiv.            PROTOZOOLOGY: This is the study of protozoa

xv.            TAXONOMY: This deals with the classification of organisms.

xvi.            VIROLOGY: This is the scientific study of viruses

 

CHARACTERISTICS OF LIVING ORGANISMS

Living organisms have some features which differentiate them from non-living organism these include,

1.       Nutrition: All living organism need food, which is used as a source of energy, and materials for the processes of life, such as growth. Only two sources of energy are used by living organisms namely.

(a)    Light Energy

(b)    Chemical Energy

Those organisms specialized for using light energy carry out photosynthesis and contain pigment, including Chlorophyll, which absorb light Plants, Algae and some organisms including Bacteria.

Those organisms which use chemical energy must obtain it from other living organisms. They include  Animal and Fungi.

2.       Respiration: All living organism respire. It is the libration of energy from food (assimilated food) by the process of oxidation. Sometimes, energy is librated using molecular oxygen. Various structures are used to obtain oxygen. In plants, respiratory structures include: (i) stomata (ii) lenticels and (iii) pneumatophores.

In animals, they include the entire body surface as in protozoan, the trachea as in insects, lung-book as in scorpion, gills as in fishes and tadpoles and lungs in adult toad, reptiles and other higher vertibrates. Sometimes, energy can be produced in the absence of oxygen (Anaerobic respiration). The energy released from food is stored in molecules of adenosine triphosphate (ATP)

It is sometimes referred to as “universal gravity carrier”

3.       Irritability: All living organism have the ability to react or respond to changing  internal or external environments. They are sensitive to their environments. In responding, animals depend on sensory, skin, tongue, etc.

Condition (stimuli) to which organisms respond include: light, touch, heat, pressure, cold etc.

 

4.    Movement: Most animals change their environment. The reason are:

(i) To look for food

(ii) To look for partners

(iii) To seek shelter and  

(iv) To escape from enemies.

On the other hand, most plants are fixed and do not move. The few plants that move are the unicellular types (e.g. Euglena, chlamydomonas). However, certain part of plants can move due to growth (called growth movement).

 

5.    Excretion: Excretion is the removal from the body of waste products of metabolism. For example, the process of aerobic respiration produces a waste product, CO2, which can be harmful in excess and must be eliminated. Others include protein deamination (in the liver), urea, a nitrogenous substance is produced.

In plants, the excretory structures include the stomata, and Lenticels.

In animals, the structures are numerous namely, contractile vacuoles (e.g. protozoa), flame cells (e.g. turbellaria, tapeworms), malpighian tubules (e.g. insects), and kidneys (as in fishes and higher vertebrates).

 

6.    Reproduction: All living organisms have the power to give rise to their kinds. Reproduction can be (i) asexual or (ii) sexual.

(i) Asexual reproduction: here, mature organism produces new individuals without mating or exchanging sex cells (gametes) with another organism. Asexual reproduction can be by

a. Budding: as in yeast cells and hydra

b. Fragmentation: as in spirogyra

c. Binary Fission: as in amoeba and paramecium.

(ii) Sexual Reproduction: here, two mature individuals, often the male and female come together and produce sex cell (gametes). Two gametes one from each parent unite (Fertilization). A zygote results, this grows to a new individual.

 

7.    Growth: growth is shown by all living organisms. It is an increase in body size or weight. The food materials absorbed into the body, provide energy. This is used for repairs and body building.
