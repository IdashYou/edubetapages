1. PUBLIC COOPERATION

Meaning of Public Corporations (Parastatals)

Public Corporations/Parastatals are companies, establishments, and enterprises owned and financed by the government to render essential social and welfare services at affordable prices. Examples of public corporations/parastatals are: Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC), Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation (NNPC), Nigerian Ports Authority (NPA), Nigerian Television Authority (NTA), Power Holding Company of Nigeria (PHCN), National Bureau of Statistics (NBS), National Teachers Institute (NTI), National Universities Commission (NUC), etc.

Public Corporations are expected to make profit or to be self-sustaining. However, profit-making is not the main aim for setting them up, the main aim is to render essential social and welfare services at affordable prices.

They are managed and supervised by a board of directors appointed by the government for a specific period of time. The administrative head is known as the Chairman.

Privatisation

Privatisation is a policy designed to enable individuals and private or corporate organisations take over the ownership and control of government businesses such as public corporations and parastatals.

Reasons for Privatisation

1. Privatisation helps to redirect resources from government businesses to other pressing areas of the economy.

2. Privatisation also helps to attract foreign investments in the economy because of attractive profits expected.

3. Privatisation helps to reduce wastage and reduce corruption in the system.

4. Privatisation helps in attracting foreign technology as well as expertise from developed economies.

NB:

Ombudsman (public complaint commission) originated from Sweden in 1809. The public complaints commission or the ombudsman is the institution established by the federal government to entertain from citizens, complaints of injustice, corruption, unfair treatment and abuse of office by public officers. A major shortcoming of the Ombudsman is lack of executive power to enforce decisions.

Moreover, one of the limitations of the powers of the Ombudsman is that it has no powers of the enforcement of its investigations and decisions it has taken. It can only investigate and recommend.

An ombudsman is a person who has been appointed to look into complaints about companies and organisations. Ombudsmen are independent, free of charge and impartial – that is, they don't take sides with either the person who is complaining or the organisation being complained about.
