Settings
Physical: The story is set primarily in Stardom Schools, Lagos, a prestigious private institution symbolizing Mr. Bepo’s dedication to education amid challenges like demanding parents, financial pressures, and staff tensions. Key events occur in the assembly hall, classrooms, and the MD’s office. Other Lagos locations, like Mushin and Lekki, reflect Nigeria’s socio-economic disparity, while Badagry evokes the history of slavery and its connection to modern migration. Natural sites like Erin Ijesha Waterfalls and Ikogosi Warm Springs celebrate Nigeria’s beauty and heritage.

Temporal: Set in contemporary Nigeria, the novel addresses modern challenges like migration (“Japa”), high education costs, and bureaucratic inefficiencies, with global parallels such as brain drain.

Psychological: Mr. Bepo struggles with internal conflicts, including societal pressures to migrate, professional ambitions, and his dedication to his students. Tensions at Stardom Schools mirror the psychological strain of job insecurity and parental influence.

Themes

  1. Migration and Identity: The allure of relocating abroad contrasts with sacrifices like losing one’s roots. Modern migration is paralleled with the legacy of slavery, symbolized by Badagry and the Black Heritage Museum.
  2. Education and Leadership: Bepo exemplifies integrity and dedication in tackling systemic issues in education, such as financial constraints and parental interference.
  3. Sacrifice and Family: Bepo’s reluctance to migrate despite family expectations reflects personal sacrifices for his students.
  4. Cultural Reflection: Excursions to historical and natural sites highlight Nigeria’s rich heritage and challenge the preference for foreign lands.
  5. Ethics and Integrity: Bepo’s rejection of unethical practices underscores his moral principles amidst societal corruption.
  6. Modern Nigerian Society: Stark inequalities, corruption, and resilience are explored through Lagos’s contrasting areas and systemic challenges.
  7. Hope and Redemption: Bepo’s choice to stay and serve his community symbolizes hope and belief in positive change.

Language/Style

  • Conversational Tone: Accessible, reflecting Nigerian English speech patterns and engaging readers.
  • Humour and Wit: Characters like Mr. Audu provide comic relief, balancing serious themes.
  • Symbolism: Badagry’s historical significance ties migration to slavery.
  • Proverbs/Local Expressions: Enrich the narrative, reflecting Nigeria’s cultural diversity (e.g., "Japa").
  • Descriptive Imagery: Vivid portrayals of natural and cultural sites evoke pride in Nigeria’s heritage.
  • Irony: Highlights societal contradictions, such as Africans migrating to colonial lands as a “new slavery.”

Chapter 1 (Dusk)

The principal of Stardom Schools, Mr. Bepo, shocked everyone during the morning assembly by walking unusually, crying uncontrollably, and being unable to speak. The vice principal, Mrs. Grace Apeh, dismissed the students and led him to his office, where he continued weeping for over 30 minutes. Rumors spread quickly among parents, prompting Mrs. Apeh to inform the Managing Director, Mrs. Ibidun Gloss, who returned to school to address the situation.

The school had started the day celebrating a recent 90% WASSCE success rate and rewarding teachers, though two were reprimanded for poor student performance. Earlier, the school had also announced a reduction in boarding fees, leading to improved punctuality among students.

Despite efforts by staff and a pastor, Mr. Bepo remained unresponsive. When attempts to reach his wife failed, the MD decided to send him home with the school’s guidance counselor to ensure his safety, prioritizing his well-being and the school’s reputation.

Chapter 2 (The Enticement)

Five days after his emotional breakdown, Mr. Bepo revealed the reason for his distress: he was relocating to the UK under pressure from his wife and children, despite his reluctance to leave his students and his plans for the future. Known as "The Lekki Headmaster" for his empathetic conflict resolution skills, Mr. Bepo had served four years as headmaster of Stardom Kiddies and the rest as principal of the secondary school. His colleagues found his hesitation to embrace the “golden opportunity” to relocate amusing and ironic.

Before the relocation, Bepo dreamed of retiring at 55, starting a school, or pursuing ventures like farming, transportation, or water production. However, these plans were now set aside as he prepared to join his family abroad for a new chapter of his life.

Chapter 3 (Migration Tales)

Bepo eventually resolved to relocate and began listening to stories from others who had already made the move. He learned about the payment systems abroad, where salaries could be hourly, daily, weekly, or monthly, unlike the fixed monthly pay common in Nigeria. Bepo particularly liked the hourly system, which allowed employees to change jobs more freely and employers to closely monitor performance. He believed this system encouraged accountability and contributed to the development of other countries.

He discovered that skilled workers earned significantly higher wages abroad, with daily earnings ranging from $150 to $250, amounting to as much as $2400 per month. This was a stark contrast to the N1600 many Nigerians earned. Skilled professions such as nursing and medicine were particularly in demand, and thousands of health workers and graduates had been emigrating annually, with many utilizing education visas to relocate.

Bepo also heard of desperate measures taken by some to fund their relocation. He recalled a colleague, Mr. Nku, who borrowed N2 million from the school’s cooperative and relocated two days later, and a school driver who attempted to sell a bus to finance his son's education abroad.

Sola, a former colleague who had relocated to the UK with her husband six months earlier, reassured Bepo about his transition since his wife was already there. She shared how she and her husband sold everything and borrowed N4 million to fund their journey. Sola explained that migrating couples often divide responsibilities, with one partner working while the other studies.

The appeal of relocating also included opportunities for children to access free primary and secondary education and quality healthcare, especially for children who receive regular checkups. Bepo compared this to the high cost of education at Stardom, where parents paid millions in fees.

However, the stories weren’t always encouraging. Jare, a former banker, regretted relocating after finding himself caring for an elderly couple in London. Another man, Hope, an accountant pursuing a Master’s degree in the UK, depended on his wife for financial support until she refused to continue. These experiences made Bepo reflect on life’s unpredictability, recalling an Idoma proverb about how sugarcane and bitter leaves derive contrasting tastes from the same rain.

The question of bringing dependents from Nigeria also arose. While some, like Riike, were eager to sponsor relatives abroad, others were hesitant, showing that everyone’s journey was shaped by their unique circumstances.
