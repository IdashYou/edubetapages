ECOLOGY
Meaning of Ecology: Ecology is the study of plants and animals in relation to their environment. Ecology is divided into two main branches:
(a) Autecology: Autecology is concerned with the study of an individual organism or a single species of organism and its environment.
(b) Synecology: is concerned with the study of the inter-relationships between groups of organisms or inter-relationships between groups of organisms or species of organism living together in an area.

ECOLOGICAL CONCEPTS
(1) ENVIRONMENT: The environment includes all the factors external and internal, living and non-living factors which affect an organism.
(2) BIOSPHERE or ECOSPHERE: Is the zone of the earth occupied by living organisms.
(3)LITHOSPHERE: is the solid portion of the earth. It is the outermost layer of zone of the earth's crust.
(4) HYDROSPHERE: Is the liquid or aquatic part of the earth. It covers about 70% or 2/3 of the earth crust.
(5) ATMOSPHERE: is the gaseous portion of the earth. It is layer of gas surrounding the earth.
(6) HABITAT: is defined as an area occupied by a biotic community. The various types of habitat are:
(a) Aquatic habitat: eg. Rivers, lakes, ponds, stream, lagoons, seas, oceans, etc.
(b) Terrestrial habitats: e.g. savanna, forest. Desert, etc.
(c) Arboreal habitats: e.g. tree top, tree trunks
(7) BIOTIC COMMUNITY OR BIOME: A biotic community is any naturally occurring group of different organisms living together and interacting in the same environment. A biome is the largest community of organisms e.g. rain forest, Guinea savanna, etc.
(8) ECOLOGICAL NICHE: Ecological niche refers to the specific portion of a habitat which is occupied by a particular-species or organism.
(9) POPULATION: Population is defined as the total number of organisms of the same species living together in a given area.
(10) COMMUNITY: Is a natural assemblage of several population of different species of plants and animals living in a particular habitat.
(11) ECOSYSTEM: An ecosystem refers to a community of plants and animals functioning together with their non-living environment. In order words, ecosystem consist of the living factor (plants and animals) interacting with the non-living factors, in an environment.

Components of an Ecosystem
The ecosystem is made up of two main components. These are:
(a) Biotic components (b) Abiotic components

(a) Biotic components: The biotic components include the living things (plants and animals) which can be grouped into:
(i) Producer (ii) consumers (iii) decomposers

(i) Producer: These are organisms which produce their own food i.e. they are Autotrophs e.g. Green plants and some certain bacteria. The green plants produce their food by using energy from sunlight, with the help of their chlorophyll. They convert solar energy to chemical energy in food through a process called photosynthesis.

(ii) Consumers: They are the animals which feed on the producer-green plants. They cannot make their foods. So they feed on plants.
(a) Primary Consumers: These are the heterotrophic animals which feed on the producers directly. They are sometimes called Herbivores e.g. Grasshoppers, cow, goat, sheep, etc.
(b) Secondary Consumers: These are the heterotrophic animals which feed on the animals that feed on producers. Examples are dogs, cat, man, etc.
(c) Tertiary Consumer: There are the heterotrophic animals that feed upon Secondary Consumers. Example include vulture, carnivores animals.
(d) Decomposers: These are organisms which feed on dead or decay plants and animals e.g. certain bacteria and fungi.

(b) Abiotic Components
An abiotic component is the non-living things which includes temperature, sunlight, rainfall, CO2, O2, nitrogen, rocks, dust, storm, fire and water, etc.

FUNCTIONS OF ECOSYSTEM
It is made up of living organisms (plants and animals) and their non-living environment.

Autotrophs:
Autotrophs are organisms (green plants and some bacteria) which can use, sunlight or chemicals to manufacture their food from inorganic substances during the process of photosynthesis.

Heterotrophs:
Heterotrophs are organisms, mainly animals, which cannot manufacture their own food but depend directly or indirectly in plants for their food, hence they are called consumers.

Decomposers
Decomposers are organisms that feed on dead organisms and other decaying organic materials. Examples of decomposers are insects such as termite, larvae of housefly, bacteria and fungi.

ADAPTATION FOR SURVIVAL
Adaptation is defined as the possession of special features or structure which improves the chances of an organism to survive in its environment.
Competition: Competition is defined as the process by which living organisms in a habitat struggle or compete with one another for such limited essential needs in the environment eg. light, nutrients, mates, shelter and. space.

Factors responsible for competition
The reasons for competition among plants include:
Inadequate (I) light (ii) space (iii) nutrients (iv) water

Reasons for competition among animals: Inadequate
(i) food (ii) space (iii) mates.

Types of Competition
There are two main types of competition. These are:
(i) Intra-specific competition: also called intra-species competition is the type of competition between individuals of the same species.
(ii) Inter-specific competition: also called inter-specific competition, is the type of competition which exist between individuals of different species. '

Structural Adaptations
Structural Adaptation are special modification of structures which help organisms to survive better in their various environment. Some examples of structural adaptations are described below.

Structural Adaptation for obtaining food
(a) By toad: (i) The toad possess special olfactory organ in the head for perceiving the odour of its food.
(ii) The tongue is attached to the front of the mouth which can easily be extended to capture or trap the prey.
(iii) The tongue is long and sticky to help hold the prey.

(b) By Birds:
Birds have structural adaptation (beaks and feet) which enable them to obtain food.

Structural adaptation for protection and defense
(1) Snakes: They attack their enemies or defend themselves by biting and injecting their venom into the victim.

Adaptive Colouration:
Adaptive colouration is the possession by an organism of a colour which enables it to catch its preys, avoid its predators or enemies and ensure their survival.
Example of Adaptive colouration in Animals

  1. Warning colouration: a warning colouration is one that scares away the animals' enemies or predators. For example, the black and yellow colour of variegated grasshopper.
  2. Batesian mimicry: is a condition in which a harmless organism resembles a distasteful or harmful one so that its enemies would avoid it.
  3. Camouflage: camouflage is a concealing colouration which matches the color of the background. Example: grasshoppers have green pigments in their cuticle and green snakes also have green pigments on their skin.
  4. Mating colouration: the conspicuous skin colouration in the adult males help the adult females to select males of their own species for mating.
Counter shading: is the possession of two distinct colours (dorsal and ventral) by some organisms to match the colour of their environment.
