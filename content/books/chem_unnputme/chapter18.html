What is Chemical Reaction?

A chemical reaction is a process in which one or more substances, also called reactants, are converted to one or more different substances, known as products

Six common types of chemical reactions are discussed below.

 

Types of Chemical Reactions

Explanation

General Reaction

Combination reaction

Two or more compounds combine to form one compound.

A + B → AB

 

2Na(s)+Cl2(g)→2NaCl(s)
 

2Mg(s)+O2(g)→2MgO(s)

Decomposition reaction

The opposite of a combination reaction – a complex molecule breaks down to make simpler ones.

AB → A + B

 

CaCO3(s)→CaO(s)+CO2(g)

 

2NaOH(s)→Na2O(s)+H2O(g)

Precipitation/Double Decomposition reaction

Two solutions of soluble salts are mixed resulting in an insoluble solid (precipitate) forming.

A + Soluble salt B  → Precipitate + soluble salt C

 

2KI(aq)+Pb(NO3)2(aq)→2KNO3(aq)+PbI2(s)

Neutralization reaction

An acid and a base react with each other. Generally, the product of this reaction is a salt and water.

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

NaOH(aq)+2HCl(aq)→NaCl(aq)+H2O(l)

Combustion reaction

Oxygen combines with a compound to form carbon dioxide and water. These reactions are exothermic, meaning they give off heat.

A + O2 → H2O + CO2

 

C3H8(g)+5O2(g)→3CO2(g)+4H2O(g)

Displacement reaction

One element takes place with another element in the compound.

A + BC → AC + B

 

Zn(s)+2HCl(aq)→ZnCl2(aq)+H2(g)

 

 

ENERGY CHANGES AND RATE OF REACTION

Enthalpy is defined as the heat content of a chemical reaction. It is the heat change at constant pressure. Enthalpy can be endothermic or exothermic. It is +ve for endothermic reaction and –ve for exothermic reaction.

Endothermic reaction is a reaction in which the energy is absorbed in the form of, while such chemical reactions in which energy is released or evolved in the form of heat is known as the exothermic reaction.

If the hydration energy is less than the lattice energy, then the chemical reaction is endothermic, (hydration energy < lattice energy).

In exothermic reaction the hydration energy is greater than the lattice energy, (hydration energy > lattice energy).

The main factors that affect the rate of chemical reaction are; temperature, pressure, surface area, concentration and the nature of reactants.

First law of thermodynamics states that for a closed system the total energy is constant. It also implies that energy can neither be created or destroyed but can be converted from one form to another. It is related to the law of conservation of energy. The expression for this is

H = E + W

The second law of thermodynamics explained the spontaneity of a reaction. A reaction can be spontaneous under the following conditions

  1. if the change of free energy is negative (H = -ve).
  2. Change in entropy can be positive or negative depending on the heat content of the reaction.

Heat of reaction can be defined as the amount of heat evolved or absorbed when a chemical reaction occurs between molar quantities of the substances as represented in the equation of reaction under standard conditions. 

The hydration energy is the heat change that takes place when one mole of a gaseous ion dissolves in water to give an infinitely dilute solution. It is associated with liberation of heat. In exothermic reaction hydration energy is always greater than lattice energy. 

Heat of neutralization is the heat change when a hydroxonium ion (H3O+(aq)) reacts with hydroxyl ion (OH-). The heat of neutralization of strong acid and strong base is constant because they ionize completely.

Activation energy is the energy reactant particle must overcome before product is formed.

If a reaction is exothermic and there is a great disorder (spontenous) there will be a large decrease in free energy.

H = Heat content of reactants – Heat content of the product

∆H = Enthalpy change is the name given to the amount of heat evolved or absorbed in a reaction carried out at constant pressure. It is +ve for endothermic reaction and –ve for exothermic reaction.

∆S = Entropy is a state function. The entropy change is determined by its initial and final states. It is +ve if the number of gaseous products is higher than that of gaseous reactant. Change in entropy is –ve or decreases when the number of moles of gaseous reactants is higher than that of the gaseous product.

Change in entropy is 0 (zero) if the number of moles gaseous reactants is equal to that of gaseous product.

Dissolution of anhydrous CuSO4 is exothermic while that of hydrated CuSO4 is endothermic.

G = gibb’s free energy is equal to zero for a reaction in equilibrium. 

NB:

Effect of Temperature

  • Increase in temperature will favour forward reaction if H = +ve (endothermic reaction), while decreasing the temperature will favour the backward reaction.
  • But if, H = -ve (an exothermic reaction), a decrease in temperature will favour the forward reaction while a decrease in temperature will favour the forward reaction.

Effect of Pressure

  • Pressure only affect gaseous species
  • If the number of moles of gaseous reactants is equal to the number of moles of gaseous product then pressure does not have effect again.
  • If the number of moles of gaseous reactants is greater than the number of moles of gaseous product, increase in in pressure favour the forward reaction, according to Boyle’s law.
  • If the number of moles of gaseous Product is greater than the number of moles of gaseous reactant, then an increase in in pressure will favour the forward reaction, according to Boyle’s law.

Entropy

In chemistry, entropy is represented by the capital letter S, and it is a thermodynamic function that describes the randomness and disorder of molecules based on the number of different arrangements available to them in a given system or reaction. 

Gases have higher entropy than liquids while solids have the least entropy.

Let’s randomly assign entropy values to Gases, liquids and solids as 3, 2 and 1 respectively

In H2O(s) → H2O(l)

Change in Entropy will be

Entropy of product - Entropy of reactant = 2 – 1 = 1 

Thus positive.

When a substance changes from liquid state to gaseous state, the attractive force between the molecules decreases. Attractive force is maximum in solid states, lesser in liquid and least in gases. 

At high temperatures, rate of reaction is high and powdered reactants has greater surface area for reactions, than a lump of reactants which has lower surface area for reactions.

JAMB Examples

The situation obtained when a perfect gas expands into a vacuum is

A. ΔH is positive and TΔ S is zero

B. ΔH is positive and T Δ S is negative

C. ΔH is negative and TΔ S is zero

D. ΔH is zero and T Δ S is positive

D. ΔH is zero and T Δ S is positive. 2018

Explanation

Formula for enthalpy change: ΔH = ΔU + Pex(ΔV)

The enthalpy change is zero because both terms on the right are zero in free expansion of an ideal gas.

There is no external pressure in a free expansion, so P (ex) = 0. And the internal energy change, ΔU of free expansion in a closed system is zero since no inter molecular forces have to be overcome. The temperature remains constant and no heat is absorbed or released.
