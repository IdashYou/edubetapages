Water is by far the most abundant substance in plant and animal tissues. Over 80% of the earth’s surface is covered by water. Water is used in everyday life like drinking, washing, cleaning, cooking, dyeing, and as a solvent in industrial processes. 

 

Water is referred to as universal solvent because it dissolves most substances.

 

Types of water

There are two types of water 

 

Natural water 

Treated water

 

1. Natural water

Natural water Includes rain water, spring water, lake, seawater and well water.

 

Rainwater is the purest form of natural water because it is formed as a result of the condensation of water vapor in the atmosphere.

 

Springwater is a good source of drinking water because it contains little suspended impurities. Well water contains a lot of clay and other minerals salts. River water, lake water, seawater contain a lot of dissolved air, mineral salt, bacterial and organic remains.

 

2. Treated water

This type of water is usually prepared for special purposes e.g. distilled water, pipe-borne water, and chlorinated water which is used in swimming pools.  

 

Water is formed when two atoms of hydrogen and one atom of oxygen are covalently bonded together. 

2H2(g)+O2(g) →2H20(l)
 

The structure of water is V-shaped and the bond angle is 1050c. This is because the two bond pair is brought about by the repulsion of two lone pairs of electrons.
 

Physical Properties of Water
 

 

  1. Pure water is a clear, colorless, tasteless, and odorless liquid
  2. It boils at 1000C and freezes at 00C
  3. It is neutral to litmus
  4. It has a maximum density of 1.00gcm-3 at 40C

 

Chemical Properties of water

  1. Reaction with Metals

Water reacts with highly reactive and fairly reactive metals under different conditions. The reaction depends on the position of metals in the electrochemical series. 

(a) Potassium and sodium react vigorously with cold water with the evolution of heat to form hydroxide and hydrogen gas.

(i) 2K(s) + 2H2O(g)  → 2KOH(aq) + H2(g)

(ii) 2Na(s) + 2H2O(g) → 2NaOH(aq)

(b) Calcium reacts steadily with cold water to form calcium hydroxide and hydrogen 

Ca(s) + 2H2O → Ca(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)

(c) Magnesium, Aluminum, and Zinc reacts with steam to form oxide and hydrogen gas 

(i} Mg(g) +H2O(g) → MgO(s) + H2(g)

(ii) 4Al(s) + 6H2O(g) → 2Al(2)O3(s) + 6H2(g)

(iii) Zn(s) +  H2O(g) →  ZnO(s) + H2(g)

(d) Iron reacts reversibly with excess stem at red hot to form oxide (Iron (III) oxide) and hydrogen gas.

3 Fe(s) + 4H2(g) → Fe3O4(s) + 4H3(g)

(e) Lead, copper, silver, mercury and gold do not react with water

2. Action of Water on Non-Metals

Non-metals like carbon, chlorine and silicon 

(a) Reaction with carbon: when steam is passed over red hot carbon, hydrogen and carbon (II) oxide are formed.

C(s) + H2O(g) → CO(g) +  H2(g)

(b) Reaction with chlorine: Chlorine reacts with water from a mixture of two acids hydrochloric acid and oxochlorate (I) acid  

Cl2(g) + H2O(l) → HCl(aq) + HOCl4(aq)

(c)Reaction with silicon: silicon reacts with steam to form silicon(iv)oxide and hydrogen gas

Si[s] + 2H2O(g) → SiO2(g) + 2H2(g)

3. Action of Water on Metal Oxide

(a) Sodium oxide and potassium oxide readily dissolve in water to form very strong hydroxide which turns red litmus paper blue

(i) Na2O(s) + H2O(l) → 2NaOH(aq)

(ii) K2O(s) + H2O(l) → 2KOH(aq)

(b) Calcium oxide (quick lime) partially dissolves in water to form calcium hydroxide (slaked lime) 

CaO(s) + H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq)

4. Action of Water on Non-Metalic Oxide

Non-metalic oxide reacts with water to give the corresponding acids. Acidic oxides are also called acid anlydrides.

An acid anhydride is an acidic oxide that dissolves in water to produce acid e.g SO2, SO3, NO2, CO2, P2O3, P2O5

(i) SO2(g) + H2O(l) → H2SO3(aq)

(ii) CO2(g) + H2O(l) → H2SO4(aq)

(iii) SO3(g) + H2O(l) → H2SO4(aq)

(iv) 2NO2(g) + H2O(l)  → HNO2(aq) + HNO3(aq)

NO2 is a mixed anhydride because it produced two acids when it reacts with water 

(v) P2O3(s) + 3H2O(1) → 2H3PO3(aq)

(vi) P2O5 + 3H2O(l)  → 2H3PO4(aq)

5. Hydrolysis

Water reacts with organic and inorganic compounds, such reactions are generally described as hydrolysis 

(i) CaC2(s) + 2H2O(l) →  Ca(OH)2(aq) + C2H2(g)

(ii) Al4C3(s) + 12H2O(l) → 4Al(OH)3(s) + 3CH4(g)

(iii) Al2S(s) + 6H2O(l) →  Al (OH)3(s) + 3H2S(g)

 

Method:

  • Add liquid to anhydrous copper (II) sulfate
  • Colour change is observed and recorded

Result:

  • If liquid is water, the white powder will turn blue
  • This is because hydrated copper (II) sulfate is formed and is a blue crystal
  • Does not indicate whether water is pure
