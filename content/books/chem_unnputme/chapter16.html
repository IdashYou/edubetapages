PERIODIC TABLE

The modem periodic law is based on atomic number while Mendeleev law is based on relative atomic masses. The modern periodic law states that the properties of the elements are a periodic function of their atomic numbers. Rutherford classified elements before Mendeleev.

The Periods

Period - the horizontal rows running from left to right in the periodic table are called the periods. Elements in the same period have the same number of electron shell.  We have 7 rows in the periodic table.o understand the numbering of the periods you need to understand quantum numbering discussed later. In the periodic table. The second row consists of 2s orbital (Li and Be) and the. In the third row is found 3p orbitals which are (Al, Si, P, S, Cl and Ar). The 4th row consists of the 3d orbitals, this because d orbitals are place one row below their energy level.

The Group

Group – is a vertical column or family, that runs from top to bottom in the periodic table. The elements in the same group have the same number of electrons on their outermost orbital. These electrons are also called valence electrons.

We will be discussing the different groups 

Group 1 (IA) - Alkali Metals (excluding H)

Members of this group includes; Li (lithium), Na (sodium), K (potassium), Rb (rubidium), Cs (cesium), and Fr (francium).

All form hydroxides (e.g.  NaOH) All the members are active metals. 

Their activity increases as you move down the column. React violently when they come into contact with water. All have one valence electron; they lose one electron to form cations with a charge e.g.

Li+, Na+, and K+

Group 2 (IIA) - Alkaline Earth Metals

Members of this group includes; Be (beryllium), Mg (magnesium), Ca (calcium), Sr (strontium), Ba (barium), and Ra (radium). All the members are active metals except Be and Mg. 

Activity increases as you move down the column. Ca, Sr, and Ba react violently when they come into contact with water. All have two valence electrons e.g. Be is less active than Li; Mg is less active than Na

They lose two electrons to form cations with a charge of +2 e.g.  Be2+, Mg2+, and Ca2+

It is important to know that alkaline earth metals are less active than their adjacent alkali metals

e.g.  Be is less active than Li; Mg is less active than Na.

Groups 3-12 (IIIA - VIIIB) - Transition Metals

All have valence electrons in the d-orbitals. They form compounds in which they have various oxidation numbers.

Ag (silver) generally forms Ag+ (+1 oxidation state) 

Zn (zinc) generally forms Zn2+ (+2 oxidation state).

Transition Metals are metallic elements in which the last electron added enters a d orbital. The valence electrons (those added after the last noble gas configuration) in these elements include the ns and (n – 1) d electrons. The official IUPAC definition of transition elements specifies those with partially filled d orbitals. Thus, the elements with completely filled orbitals (Zn, Cd, Hg, as well as Cu, Ag, and Au are not technically transition elements. However, the term is frequently used to refer to the entire d block.

Group 13 (IIIB)

The group has semimetal and nonmetal characteristics. B (boron) – is the only element in the group that is not a metal; Al (aluminum) – is fairly an active metal, third most abundant in the earth's crust.

They lose three electrons to form Al3+ to form an ion.

Forms compounds in which it has an oxidation state of +3

Other metals - Ga (gallium), In (indium), and Tl (thallium) - very scarce active metals

Group 14 (IVB)

A notable member of this group is carbon (C) which is a nonmetal. Carbon has a strong C-C single bonds, C=C double bonds, and CC triple bonds. 

Carbon: It forms covalent bonds with other elements and can form double and triple bonds with other nonmetals.

Elemental forms of carbon include:

Graphite (crystalline) - Strong bonds between atoms within planes resulting in extremely high melting and boiling points.  Weaker bonds connecting the planes which account for the soft texture of graphite. 

Diamond (crystalline) - Hardest naturally occurring substance with extremely high melting and boiling points.  Atoms arranged in a tetrahedral array with strong C-C bonds (tetrahedral shape). 

Charcoal - Results from heating wood without oxygen present. Charcoal is used in purification and absprbtion of colours. 

Coke (amorphous) - More structured than other amorphous forms of carbon; made from coal. 

Carbon Black (amorphous) - Formed by burning natural gas or other carbon compounds in a limited amount of air

Silicon (S) and Germanium (Ge): they are known to be semimetals. Silicon is the second most abundant element in the earth's crust. 

Tin (Sn) and Lead (Pb) are also less reactive metals. The both form compounds in which their oxidation states are +2 or +4.

Bronze is an alloy of tin and copper.

Group 15 (VB)

Nitrogen (N) and Phosphorus (P) are notable members of this groups and they are nonmetals. 

Nitrogen: Nitrogen is found in its elemental form at room temperature as a diatomic gas (N2). 
Nitrogen makes up approximately 80% of earth's atmosphere by volume. In compounds, the oxidation of nitrogen can range from -3 to +5. 
Haber process - mixing N2 and H2 gases at 200 to 300 atm and 400C to 600 over a finely divided iron catalyst to produce NH3.
Phosphorus: Pure elemental phosphorus is white phosphorus (P4).  It is highly reactive and combusts with air at room temperature but is unreactive with water. 
Red phosphorus is formed when white phosphorus is heated and is much less reactive than white phosphorus. 
Phosphorus can expand its valence (outermost) shell to hold more than eight electrons (can store extra electrons in the 3d orbitals). 
N  N triple bonds are much stronger than P  P. 
Moreover, P-P single bonds are stronger than N-N single bonds.

Arsenic (As) and Antimony (Sb): – they are semimetals 
Bi (bismuth): – is a metal

Group 16 (VIB)

Members of this group are either nonmetals or semi-metals.

Oxygen (O), Sulfur (S), and Selenium (Se) are the nonmetals in the group.

Oxygen: oxygen is the most abundant element on earth, making up approximately 45% of the earth's crust (by weight), 85% of the oceans (by weight) and 20% of the atmosphere (by volume). 
Oxygen is generally diatomic (O2) in its elemental form, but ozone (O3) is an allotrope of O2.

Ozone is the only known allotrope of oxygen, and it is toxic at a concentration above 1 ppm. Ozone can absorb ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun, and serves as a filter in the atmosphere (Ozone layer). Oxygen is a very strong oxidizing agent, weaker only to fluorine (Oxidation can also be defined as the gain of oxygen by a compound). Oxygen generally takes on a ‘-2’ oxidation number in compounds.

Note that in peroxides (O22-), oxygen has a ‘-1’ oxidation number, e.g. H2O2, hydrogen peroxide.

Sulphur: Sulphur is a yellow solid at room temperature with a cyclical molecular structure (S8). 
O=O double bonds are much stronger than S=S double bonds. 
S-S single bonds are stronger than O-O bonds. 
Sulfur can expand its valence (outermost) shell, to hold more than eight electrons (can store extra electrons in the 3d orbitals). 
In compounds, sulfur can have oxidation numbers ranging from -2 to +6. 
The prefix thio- is given to compounds in which an S atom replaces an O atom.

Tellurium (Te) and Polonium (Po): they are members of the group 15 (VB) that are semimetals.

Group 17 (VIIB) - Halogens

This group is known as the Halogen group, and they include; Fluorine (F), Chlorine (Cl), Bromine (Br), Iodine (I), and Astatine (At).
All Halogens are nonmetals except for Astatine (At) which is a semimetal. 
They are diatomic in their elemental form.
They gain one electron to form anions with a charge of -1.

e.g.  F-, Cl-, and Br-

In compounds, their oxidation numbers range from -1 to +7. 
because of their high reactivity, they are not found in nature in their elemental forms; but are found as salts of the halide ions. 

Chemical properties.

Their reactivity decreases down the group. At room temperature, they have the following properties.

Fluorine (F2): Fluorine is highly reactive and toxic, is the most reactive element known, and also a colorless gas.

It is so reactive to the extent that it can form compounds with noble gases (once thought to be inert) which have already achieved their octet state.
it is used in manufacturing Teflon, (C2F4) n 
it is also used to make freons which are used in refrigerators

Chlorine (Cl2): Chlorine is highly toxic and a strong oxidizing agent. It is pale yellowish green gas.

Chlorine is used commercially as a bleaching agent an as a disinfectant.

Bromine (Br2) – Bromine is a reddish-orange liquid with a bad odor. Its name is derived from the Greek word bromos which means "stench".
Bromine is used in preparing fire-extinguishing agents, sedatives, insecticides, and antiknock agents for gasoline.

Iodine (I) -Iodine is a deep purple solid with a metallic-like luster. It is easily separated from mixture by sublimation.

Solid Iodine sublimes directly into a gas (I2), i.e. when heated, it moves from solid phase to gas phase without passing through the liquid phase. 
Iodine is used as a disinfectant, catalysts, drugs, and dyes. 
When combined with silver (silver iodide (AgI)), it is used in photography.  
Biologically, Iodine deficiency in the human body can lead to a goiter, a swelling of the thyroid gland.

Group 18 (VIIIB) - Noble (Rare) Gases

This is known as the noble gas group. Members of the group includes Helium (He) meaning "sun", Neon (Ne) meaning "new one", Argon (Ar) meaning "lazy one", Krypton (Kr) meaning "hidden one", Xenon (Xe) meaning "stranger", and Radon (Ra). 
Compounds Kr, Xe, and Rn have been isolated, but none containing He, Ne, or Ar have been isolated. 
The oxidation numbers of the rare gases in compounds include +1, +2, +4, +6, and +8.

PERIODIC TRENDS

1. Ionization Energy

Ionization energy is the energy required to remove the outermost (highest energy) electron from a neutral atom in its ground state.

1. As you move down a group, ionization energy decreases.

2. As you move across a period, ionization energy increases.

Reason

As we move down the group, Electrons are further from the nucleus and thus easier to remove the outermost one (lesser ionization energy will be needed). "SHIELDING" - Inner electrons at lower energy levels essentially block the protons' force of attraction toward the nucleus.  It therefore becomes easier to remove the outer electron.

Moreover, as you move across a period (from left to right), the atomic radius decreases, that is, the atom is smaller.  The outer electrons are closer to the nucleus and more strongly attracted to the center.  Therefore, it becomes more difficult to remove the outermost electron (more and more ionization energy will be needed.

Ionization energy is affected by the following factors

  1. Screening effect
  2. Orbital penetration
  3. Size of the nuclear charge
  4. Atomic radius

2. Electron Affinity

Electron affinity is the energy change that occurs when a gaseous atom acquires an electron to form a negative ion. It can be defined as the energy given off when a neutral atom in the gas phase gains an extra electron to form a negatively charged ion.

a. As you move down a group, electron affinity decreases.

b. As you move across a period, electron affinity increases.

3. Atomic Radii

a. As you move down a group, atomic radius increases.

b. As you move across a period, atomic radius decreases.

Reason

As you move down the group the number of energy levels increases while the number of electrons increases.  Each subsequent energy level is further from the nucleus than the last.  Therefore, the atomic radius increases as the group and energy levels increase.

Moreover, as you move across a period (from left to right), electrons are added to the same energy level.  At the same time, protons are being added to the nucleus.  The concentration of more protons in the nucleus creates a higher effective nuclear charge."  As a result, there will be a stronger force of attraction pulling the electrons closer to the nucleus and thereby reducing the atomic radius.

4. Ionic Radii

a. Anions (negative ions) as found in non-metals have a larger atomic radius when compared to their unionized state.

b. Cations (positive ions) as found in metals have a smaller atomic radius when compared to their unionized state. 

Reason

Generally, the ionic radius of metals is less than their atomic radii. This is as due to the fact that metals loose electrons to achieve the octet state. This creates a larger positive charge in the nucleus than the negative charge in the electron cloud, causing the electron cloud to be drawn a little closer to the nucleus as an ion. 

In Non-metals - the atomic radius of a non-metal is generally smaller than the ionic radius of the same element. Why? Generally, non-metals loose electrons to achieve the octet. This creates a larger negative charge in the electron cloud than positive charge in the nucleus, causing the electron cloud to 'puff out' a little bit as an ion.

5. Lattice Energy

Lattice energy is the energy given off when oppositely charged ions in the gas phase come together to form a solid.

1. As you move down a group, lattice energy decreases.

2. As you increase the magnitude of the charge (becomes more positive or more negative), lattice energy increases.

Reason

As we move down the group the atomic radius increases.  To better understand this you need to know the Coulomb’s law (Coulomb's Law - The force of attraction between oppositely charged particles is directly proportional to the product of the charges of the particles (q1 and q2) and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between the particles).

Therefore, since the square of the distance is inversely proportional to the force of attraction, lattice energy decreases as the atomic radius increases.

Moreover, an increase in magnitude of charge increase lattice energy because the product of the charges of the particles is directly proportional to the force of attraction. Therefore, lattice energy increases as the charges increase.

Metallic character

1. As you move across a period, metallic character decreases and nonmetallic character increases.

2. As you move down a group, metallic character increases and nonmetallic character decreases.

In Summary

Once a compound or element has the tendency to attain noble gas structure, the compound will undergo chemical reaction.

As you move down a column or group, the ionic radius increases. This is because each row adds a new electron shell. Ionic radius decreases moving from left to right across a row or period.

Ionization potential increases across the period, from left to right and decreases down the group.

Electronegativity also increases across the period, from left to right and decreases down the group.

Electron affinity also across the period, from left to right and decreases down the group.

But the atomic radius decreases across the period and increases down the group because of the increase in the number of shells down the group.

This creates a larger positive charge in the nucleus than the negative charge in the electron cloud, causing the electron cloud to be drawn a little closer to the nucleus as an ion. 

In Non-metals - the atomic radius of a non-metal is generally smaller than the ionic radius of the same element. Why? Generally, non-metals loose electrons to achieve the octet. This creates a larger negative charge in the electron cloud than positive charge in the nucleus, causing the electron cloud to 'puff out' a little bit as an ion. 

Cations or anions of different elements are said to be isoelectronic if they both have the same electronic configuration e.g. K+ and Ca 2+ For isoelectronic cations, the more positive the ionic charge, the smaller the ionic radius. For isoelectronic anions, the more negative the ionic charge, the larger the ionic radius.

Electron affinity is the energy change that occurs when a gaseous atom acquires an electron to form a negative ion.

Ionization energy deals with the loss of electron while electron affinity deals with the gain of electron.
