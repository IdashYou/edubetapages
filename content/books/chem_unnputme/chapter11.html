Mass concentration: the mass concentration of a compound means the amount of solute present in a given volume of a solution. It is expressed in g/dm3 (gdm-3).

Mass concentration = molar concentration X molar mass.

Molar Concentration (Number of Moles): Molar concentration (M) is the number of moles of solute per dm3 of solution. It is also defined as the concentration of the solute per dm3.

No of Mole = 

This means that to calculate the number of moles in a chemical substance, you divide the general mass of the substance by the molar mass. E.g. oxygen gas, the molar mass of O2 = 16 + 16 = 32g/mol. Let’s take for instance that the giving mass (reacting mass) is 320g. Then the number of moles is 320/32= 10 moles of O2.

Molarity in mol/dm3 = 

Molarity can also be referred to as the concentration. It compares the number of moles as given above to the volume.

Let’s assume that the 10 moles of oxygen gas were in a cylinder of volume 1000dm3

Standard solution: a solution is said to be standard solution if the concentration is known.

Molar solution: A solution is said to be a molar solution if it contains one mole or the molar mass of the compound in one dm3.

PERCENTAGE YIELD & PERCENTAGE PURITY

In reality Chemistry is known as a wasteful science; this is because no reaction is 100% efficient. When we mix reactants, not 100% will react, giving us less product than expected. In other words, the actual mass of product we get is a percentage of the theoretical mass of product calculated using stoichiometry. We call this percentage the percentage yield.

A low percentage yield may be because the reaction is incomplete, like the partial dissociation of weak acids in water. There may also be side reactions to form unwanted side products. Other times, the problem lies with the laboratory technique: we lose some products during the separation and purification steps. Lower yield might also be due to the presence of impurities in the reactants or raw materials. 

Formula for percentage yield

percentage yield = actual yield ÷ theoretical yield × 100%

Here, the actual and theoretical yields could be expressed in:

  • Number of moles
  • Mass (usually for solid products)
  • Gaseous volume (usually for gas products)

Example: calculating the percentage yield of salt preparation

In this example, we will be finding the percentage yield of silver iodide formed by precipitation.

10 cm3 of 1 mol/dm3 silver nitrate solution was added to an excess of potassium iodide solution. The silver iodide precipitated was filtered, dried, and weighed. The measured mass was 2 g.

STEP 1: Write the chemical equation

AgNO3 + KI  AgI + KNO3

STEP 2: Use the amount of limiting reactant to calculate the theoretical yield of product

No. of moles of AgNO3 = concentration × volume in dm3 = 1 × (10/1000) = 0.01 mol

Mole ratio of AgNO3 to AgI = 1:1

No. of moles of AgI = 1/1 × 0.01 = 0.01 mol
Molar mass of AgI = 108 + 127 = 235 g/mol
Theoretical yield of AgI (in mass) = 0.01 × 235 = 2.35 g

STEP 3: Find the percentage mass

Actual yield of AgI = 2 g (given in question)

% yield = (2/2.35) × 100% = 85.1% 

PERCENTAGE IMPURITY

In an impure compound, part of the total mass includes the mass of the impurities in the impure compound. This means that the actual mass of the reactants is lower than expected, this leads to lower mass of product.

We can quantify how pure a sample is by its percentage purity. It is the ratio of the mass of the pure substance to the mass of the impure sample, but expressed in percentage. In the picture above, a 10% percentage purity would translate to 0.2 g of reactant in 2 g of the impure sample.

Formula for percentage purity

percentage purity = mass of pure substance ÷ mass of impure sample × 100%

The percentage of purity = 

To find the mass of pure substance, we can react a sample and leave the impurities behind. From the amount of product formed, we can back calculate to find out the amount of the pure substance that actually reacted.

Example: calculating the percentage purity of an impure sample of calcium carbonate

In this example, we will calculate the percentage purity of an impure sample containing calcium carbonate. 10 g of the impure sample is heated to decompose any calcium carbonate into calcium oxide and carbon dioxide. Only 2 dm3 of carbon dioxide was formed.

STEP 1: Write the chemical equation

CaCO3  CaO + CO2

STEP 2: Work backwards to calculate the mass of pure calcium carbonate

No. of moles of CO2 = gaseous volume ÷ molar volume = 2 ÷ 24 = 0.0833 mol

Mole ratio of CO2 to CaCO3 = 1:1

No. of moles of CaCO3 = (1/1) × 0.0833 = 0.0833 mol
Molar mass of CaCO3 = 40 + 12 + 16×3 = 100 g/mol
Mass of pure CaCO3 = 0.0833 × 100 = 8.3 g

STEP 3: Find the percentage purity

Mass of impure sample = 10 g (given in question)

Percentage purity = (8.33/10) × 100% = 83.3%
