Solution

A solution means a homogenous mixture of two or more substances. We have gaseous liquid and solid solutions, but liquid solutions are the most available type of solution.

Solute 

A solute is the substance which is being dissolved in a solution. Salt (NaCl) is a solute which can dissolve in water to give us a salt solution

Solvent

A solvent is a medium which dissolves a solute to give us a solution. Water is known as general solvent and the most common solvent in nature.

Solute + Solvent  Solution

Water is a universal Solvent which is basically due to its structure which is known to be polar (one side of the molecule is positively charged while the other side is negatively charged). 

This polar structure gives it the ability to act as excellent solvent for both covalent and electrovalent compounds. 

But generally, the first rule of solubility states that, Polar substances dissolve only in polar solvents while non polar substances are soluble in polar solvent.

Suspension 

Suspension is a heterogenous mixture of undissolved particles in a given medium. A solid is said to be in suspension in a liquid when the small particles of the solids remains undissolved in the given liquid. The liquid is known as the dispersion media while the small particles of the solids is said to be the dispersed media. An example of this is the Muddy water. 

Colloids:

A colloidal or false solution is seen as an intermediate between a suspension and a tue solution. The individual solute particles are larger than the particles of the true solution but not large enough to be seen by the naked eyes. Just like a true solution the solute can be in solid, liquid or gas state. An example of Colloid is seen in the preparation of starch with hot water, glue or jelly. A fog and even a living cell are examples of a colloid. Another example of a colloid is Emulsion milk and Albumen.

NB:

A true solution can pass through a filter paper without living any residue while a false solution cannot. 

A true Solution can be dialysed, they can pass through semipermeable membrane while a false solution cannot.

A true solution does not scatter light rays passing through it unlike colloids or suspensions which do scatter light rays. Only false solution can scatter light rays and exhibit Tyndall effect. 

The main difference between a colloid and a suspension is that colloids are very tiny and cannot be separated with filter paper and do not settle down easily on standing, but colloids are not small enough to be termed true solution.

Solubility

Solubility in simple terms is the property of substances (called the solvent) to dissolve the solutes. For example, salt is soluble in water. Here water is the solvent because it dissolves the salt (solute).

We therefore define solubility of a given solute in a particular solvent as the amount of solutes in moles or grams that will dissolve in 1dm3 of the solvent at a particular temperature.

 

Saturated Solutions

A solution is said to be saturated when it contains as much solute as it can dissolve at a particular temperature in the presence of undissolved solute particles. In this condition the dissolved solutes and the undissolved solutes are in equilibrium at the given temperature. An increase in temperature will cause more solute to dissolve thereby increasing the solubility. But a decrease in temperature will decrease the amount of dissolved solutes (excess solutes) thereby leading to decrease in the solubility.

Thus, factors that affects the concentration of saturated solution includes

1. the solute

2. the solvent 

3. the given temperature

Unsaturated solution

An unsaturated solution is a solution that has a lower concentration of solutes than it can dissolve at a particular temperature without the presence of undissolved solutes. So, an unsaturated solution can readily dissolve more solutes if they are added.

Super Saturated Solution

A supersaturated solution is a solution that contain more of a solute that it can normally dissolve at that temperature. It is an unstable solution and the excess solute will separate out if tiny crystals of solute are added to it or if the solution is disturbed.

Molarity

The term molarity or molar concentration refers to the number of moles of solute present in one litre (dm³) of its solution. 

Moles on the other hand, is the mass of a substance divided by the molar mass, as discussed in the previous chapter

No of moles = mass of substance / molar mass   

Let’s say a beaker of hydrogen chloride acid in the laboratory is labeled 0.5M. This number represents the molarity of the acid.

What it means is that 0.5 moles of hydrogen chloride gas is present in 1 litre of the solution. 

Since molar mass of HCl is 36.5g/mol, a total of 36.5 × 0.5mol. of HCl is present in 1 litre of the solution ie 

36.5 × ½mol of HCl is present in 1 litre of the solution

18.25g of HCl gas is present in 1 litre of solution.

The latter is called mass concentration. Here, we ask how many grams of a solute is present in 1 litre of its solution. 

From the example above, the molar concentration of HCl acid is 0.5M while the mass concentration is 18.25g/dm³

Difference between Molarity and Molality

Molarity and molality both are used to express the concentration of the solute. Again, despite being notoriously similar in their spelling,

Molarity (aka Molar concentration) is the number of moles of the solute that is dissolved in 1 Litre of the

solution, hence its expressed as

(number of moles) / volume of solution (in Litres)

Its unit is mol/L or M (molar)

Molality is the concentration of the solution expressed as the number of moles per kilogram of solvent.

(number of moles) / kilogram of solvent

Its unit is mol/kg or m

From the expression above, the denominator in molality is about the solvent, not the solution as in the case of molarity. Some prefer to use molality over molarity because it uses the weight (in the denominator) which doesn’t change with an increase in temperature and pressure, whereas volume (molarity) can change.

Water that contains calcium and magnesium ions is called hard water. When these ions are accompanied by hydrogen carbonate ions, the water is said to contain temporary hardness. It is called temporary because the calcium and magnesium ions can be removed from the water by boiling, addition of slaked lime or addition of washing soda, Na2CO3. 

Permanent hardness of water can only be removed by using chemicals. It is caused by the presence of calcium and magnesium ions in the form of soluble tetraoxosulphates(Vl) and chlorides. 

Soluble Salts

Below is a list of all the soluble salts:

(a) All Na, K and NH3 salts are soluble ie every compound of Na, K or NH3 eg NaCl (table salt), KCO3, NH4SO4 etc. 

(b) All nitrates eg Ca(NO3)2, Al(NO3)3, NaNO3, Mg(NO3)2 etc. 

(c) All chlorides except lead (II) chloride PbCl2 and silver chloride AgCl. 

(d) All sulphates except lead (II) sulphate PbSO4 and barium sulphate BaSO4. Note that calcium sulphate CaSO4 is ONLY slightly soluble in water.

(e) Finally, all carbonates are insoluble in water except of course the trioxocarbonates of Na, K and NH3 (Refer to (a) if in doubt)

 

JAMB Examples

(1) Calculate the solubility in mol dm-3 of 40g of CuSO4 dissolved in 100g of water at 120oC. 

[Cu = 64, S = 32, O = 16]
A. 4.00
B. 2.50
C. 0.40
D. 0.25

Answer B. 2010

Solution

Solubility in mol/dm³ = mass/molar mass × 1000/volume

Molar mass of CuSO4 = 64 + 32 + (16×4) 

ie 160g/mol

Mass of CuSO4 = 40g

Volume of 100g of water = 100 × 1 ie 100 cm³ (volume = mass × density; density of water = 1g/cm³)

Solubility = 40/160 × 1000/100 

Solubility = 2.5 mol/dm³ Option B 2010

5. The solubility of the solids that dissolves in a given solvent with the liberation of heat will

A. increase with an increase in temperature

B. decrease with an increase in temperature

C. decrease with a decrease in temperature

D. not be affected by changes in temperature

A. increase with an increase in temperature. 2018. 

Explanation

For many solids dissolved in liquid water, the solubility increases with temperature. The increase in kinetic energy that comes with higher temperature allows the solvent molecules to more effectively break apart the solute molecules that are held together by intermolecular attractions.
