12. BUSINESS ORGANIZATION

Business Organization is an undertaking which involves investment of fund and risks with the aim of providing goods and services. 

Business organization includes both the firm and the industry. Which may be owned by private individuals (private enterprise) or owned by the government (public enterprise). 

Sometimes both the government and individuals can jointly own and run a business, enterprise. This. is called joint enterprise.

Firm

A firm is the basic unit within which factors of production are organized for the purpose of producing wealth or goods and services. A firm is an independently administered business unit that carries out production of goods and services. It is an arm of an industry that is engaged in the production of a particular brand of a commodity. 

Examples of firms are Innoson Plc., First Bank Plc.etc. 

Plant (factory)

A plant is a place where a firm carries out its productive activities, it contains the tools and other equipment used in production activities.

Industry

An industry is a group of firms producing similar products. Examples are oil industries, milk industries, textile industries etc. Milk industry in Nigeria comprises of Cowbell milk, Peak milk, Nestle milk, Dano milk etc.

Classification of Business Organization

They are broadly classified into two

  1. Private enterprises
  2. Public enterprises

Types of Business Enterprises 

The major types of business enterprises are: 

  1. Sole-proprietorship
  2. The partnership
  3. The limited liability companies/joint stock companies (Private and Public).
  4. Co-operative Societies
  5. Public enterprises
  6. Joint enterprise.

Private Enterprise

Private enterprises are forms of business organization owned and run by private individuals (one or more persons) with the aim of making profits. Examples are sole Proprietorship, partnership, limited liability companies and co-operative societies. 

Public Enterprise

Public enterprises are business enterprises owned and run by the government or its agencies. They are not profit-making organizations. The main aim of establishment is to 'provide essential goods and services at affordable prices. This is to ensure that the welfare of the people increases. Examples: Public corporations, Marketing Boards, Welfare Service Boards or Commission etc. 

Features of Private Enterprises

  1. Private ownership
  2. The aim of private enterprises is to maximize profits.
  3. The management and organization are undertaken by private individuals.
  4. The owners bear the risk

The Sole-Proprietorship (One Man Business) 

The sole proprietorship is a type of business organization that is owned and controlled by a single person. He may be helped by his relatives in the running of the business.

Features of Sole-Proprietorship

  1. One-man business
  2. Capital are sourced from saving and borrowing from friends and bank.
  3. He bears the risk alone
  4. Not a limited liability and thus, private property could be sold upon business failure.
  5. Not a legal entity
  6. No board of directors

Merits

  1. Easy decision making
  2. Easy management
  3. Close relationship between owner and customers
  4. Small capital requirement, easy to set up.

Demerits

  1. Uncertainty of continuity
  2. Unlimited liability
  3. Limited capital
  4. Not a legal entity
  5. No specialization and division of labour

The Partnership 

The partnership is a form of business organization in which two or more persons combine their resources together to run a business in order to make profits. They share in the risks and profits of the business. In ordinary business, membership ranges from 2 to 20 while in banking; it is from 2 to 10. Partnership is set by a written agreement which is called “the Deed of Partnership.

Features of Partnership

  1. Membership ranges from 2 to 20 while in banking business
  2. Each member contributes capital and skill
  3. Decision is made by partners
  4. Main aim is to make profit
  5. No board of directors
  6. Not a legal entity

Merits

  1. Easy to raise capital
  2. Partners share risk
  3. Greater continuity

Demerits

  1. Delay in decision making
  2. Division of members can end the business
  3. Unlimited liability
  4. Limited capital
  5. Not a legal entity
  6. No specialization and division of labour

Types of Partnership

We have two basic types of partnership which are the ordinary partnership and the limited partnership.

The Ordinary Partnership: this is the type of partnership in which the partners have equal responsibility and bear all the risks involved in the business equally. They have equal rights and shares the profit equally.

Limited Partnership: this is the type of partnership in which the partners do not have equal responsibility. They do not share their risk equally because the liability of each partner is dependent on the capital he contributed.

Kinds of Partners

Active Partner: An active partner takes active part in the formation, financing and management of the partnership business.

Sleeping Partner (Dormant Partner): A sleeping Partner does not take part in the management and organization of the business but contributes part of the capital used in the formation of the partnership business. He shares part in the sharing of profits and losses of the business. 

Nominal Partner (Passive Partner): A nominal partner does not contribute anything to the business but his name. Nominal Partners are usually influential people in the society like Politicians, Religious Leaders, Notable Business men whose influence contributes to the growth of the company. They also share in the profit and liabilities as documented in the partnership deed provided.

 

Limited Liability Companies or Joint-Stock Companies 

Limited Liability Company is a form of business organization farmed by two or more persons who contribute their resources together through the purchase of shares with. the aim of making profits. The owners of the business are called the shareholders. 

Board of directors is elected to manage the business. 

A limited liability company is formed by registering with the registrar of companies with two legal documents 

  1. Memorandum of Association which regulates the behaviour of the business with the outside world its particulars.
  2. Articles of association which sets out the internal arrangements for the management of the business.

The joint-stock company is divided into two

Private limited liability companies

Public limited liability companies

Difference between Private and Public limited liability company

1. Membership ranges from 2 to 50 in private limited company but it is from 7 and above in public limited company. 

2. Shareholders in public limited company can easily transfer their shares without the consents of other shareholders but in a private limited company, it is not so. Employees of a public limited company can easily buy shares in the company but they cannot buy shares in a private limited company. 

3. In public limited company, shares can be quoted in the stock exchange market while it is not so in a private limited company. 

4. The accounts of a public limited company are made public while the accounts of a private limited company are not publicized to the public. 

Difference Between Shareholders and Bondholders

Shareholders are those who own stock in a company, whereas bondholders are those who own bonds issued by a company. 

In terms of Risks

Both shareholders and bondholders face certain levels of risks when they choose to invest in a given company. Those who own stock in a company run the risk of having share prices fall due to poor earnings, negative news related to the issuing company, or general market fluctuations. Furthermore, if a company performs poorly, it may opt not to issue dividends, thus eliminating a source of income for its investors.

Like shareholders, bondholders run the risk of bond values falling due to factors like poor financial performance, negative press, or general market conditions. Additionally, a company might fail to make scheduled payments, including interest payments and the repayment of principal at maturity, if it does not have enough cash on hand to fulfill its obligations as specified in its bond contract. This is known as a default.

Who takes priority during Bankruptcy?

In the event of a corporate bankruptcy, bondholders take priority in terms of repayment. Once bondholders are paid, preferred shareholders are next in line. Those who own shares of common stock are last to be paid, and for this reason, common stock is generally considered to be the riskiest way to invest in a company, while bonds are considered the least risky. No matter how you choose to invest in a company, however, the potential for monetary loss can't be ruled out.

Co-Operative Societies 

A co-operative society is a type of business organization formed by producers or consumers with common interest in order to protect their interests. 

Features of Co-Operative Societies 

  1. Profit of the business is shared on the basis of patronage.
  2. The shares of members are not transferable.
  3. Co-operative society is a Democratic form of business organization. Its voting system is one man one vote.
  4. They are not taxed.
  5. There is no limit to the number of members.
  6. The members have unlimited liability.
  7. Initial capital is raised through the sale of shares to the members in the form of pooling their resources together.
  8. The business is not a legal entity.

Types of Co-operative Societies 

In West Africa, there are three types of Co-operative societies. 

1. The Consumers’ co-operatives: these are consumers who pools their resources together in order to be able to buy goods in bulk direct from the manufacturers at cheaper rate.  

2. The Producers’ co-operatives: these are producers who have come together to in other to promote the production and sale of their products. They can pull resources together to buy or hire equipment that will be of benefit to all their members.

3. The Credit and thrift co-operatives: this is an association of people who saves money together to help members to make savings and to help members in need of money when the need arises instead of wasting money in obtaining loans from the bank.

Merits

  1. Goods are sold cheaply to members
  2. Credit and thrift cooperative help members develop habit of savings
  3. Increases standard of living of members
  4. Source of loans to members
  5. It encourages savings
  6. They are democratic
  7. They fight inflation and deflation by stabilizing price and quality.
  8. They prevent price fluctuations in the market

Demerits

  1. Inexperienced management
  2. Delayed decision making
  3. Unlimited liability
  4. Corruption and Fraud
  5. Political problems as a result of mixing politics and their activities
  6. Lack of discipline among members

The Public Enterprise 

Public enterprises are large scale business units owned and managed by government or state, with the main aim of providing goods and services to the public at cheaper rates. They are set up by law. They can also be called Public Corporations, Authority and Boards. 

NB: Public corporation is different from public limited liability company discussed earlier.

Examples of public enterprises are; Power Holding Company of Nigeria (PHCN), Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation (NNPC), Enugu State Broadcasting Corporation, etc. 

Statutory corporations are public enterprises brought into existence by a special act of parliament which defines its power and functions, rules and regulation governing its employees and its relationship with different departments in the organization. 

Features of Public Enterprises 

1. The main objective of public enterprises is to increase the welfare of the citizens at cheap rate and not profit maximization.

2. They are set up by law or act of parliament and they are owned by government. 

3. Government provides the capital.

4. Appointed Board of directors

5. They are legal entities

6. Government bears the risk.

Merits

  1. Enjoys monopoly
  2. Creates employment
  3. Source of government revenue
  4. Continuity is enabled
  5. They are source of government revenue

Demerits

  1. There is no personal relationship with customers
  2. Political instability due to changes in leaders.
  3. Poor management
  4. Corrupt practices

Differences Between Private and Public Enterprises 

1. Private enterprises are businesses owned and managed by private individuals while Public enterprises are owned and controlled by the government.

2. The capital used by private enterprise are provided by the private individuals who also bears the risk while the government provides capital for public enterprises and also bears their risk. 

3. The primary goal of private enterprise is to make profit while public enterprise aims at providing essential services.

4. Monopoly is enjoyed by public enterprise unlike private enterprise which cannot carryout monopoly.

5. Tax payers bears the losses by public enterprise while the private owners bears the loss of private enterprise.

6. There is high efficiency among private enterprise when compared with public enterprise. This is the reason why Government sometimes privatize some public sectors for more efficiency.

7. Public enterprise are established by acts of parliament (e.g. public corporations) while private enterprises are just simply registered or incorporated.

Reasons for Many Private Enterprises in Nigeria and West African Countries

1. Free Economy (Laissez faire Economy): Private individuals are allowed to participate in economic activities

2. High Efficiency of Private enterprise: the efficiency of private enterprise attracts more customer towards them.

3. Small Capital is needed for Startup: starting private enterprise does not need large capital as seen I public enterprise.

4. Many Entrepreneurs: there are many entrpreneours who are ready to take the risk of starting-up businesses. 

5. Foreign Exchange: foreign exchange is allowed in West African Countries.

6. Availability of Grants: the government makes a reasonable amount of grant available for start-up.

7. Good Business Environment: many social amenities and policies have been made to encourage private sectors including privatizing some public enterprise. 

Why Many Small Business Units Still Exist in West Africa In spite of Advantages That Large Firms have.

1. Insufficient Capital to start big firms

2. Low savings

3. Lack of Good Entrepreneurs who can take high risks.

4. Poor Economic condition

5. Inadequate Skilled Personnel

6. Underdeveloped Markets

7. Irregular Power Supply for effective production

8. Low level of Technology

9. Over reliance on foreign Goods and services

10. Inadequate Social Amenities

Quoted and unquoted Companies 

Quoted companies are business enterprises whose shares are bought and sold at the stock exchange market. Examples include all public limited liability companies. 

Unquoted companies are business enterprises whose shares are not traded on the stock exchange. market. Examples include all private limited liability companies.
