23. INTERNATIONAL TRADE

What is international Trade? International trade also known as foreign or extremal trade, is a kind of trade that involves the exchange, buying and selling of goods and services between two or more countries.

Forms of International Trade

There are two basic forms of international trade which are;

1. Bilateral International Trade: this is a form of international trade that involves the buying and selling of goods and services between two countries only.

2. Multilateral International Trade: this is a form of international trade that involves the buying and selling of goods and services among more than two countries. This is the most common form of international trade.

Reasons for International trade

1. Inequitable distribution of natural resources among nations

2. The difference in climatic conditions among nations.

3. difference in the level of technology and skills.

4. Difference in the cost of production. 

5. The Quantity and quality of labour force 

Problems of International Trade

  1. Transportation problem due to distance
  2. Communication problem due to difference in language
  3. Cultural problem
  4. Political problem
  5. Artificial barriers by the laws of the countries involved
  6. Trade imbalance between countries
  7. Negative attitude to foreigners
  8. Legal problem due to difference in the interpretation of Law

Internal Trade

This also known as domestic or home trade, it simply means the exchange, buying and selling of goods and services within a particular country.

Advantages of Internal trade

  1. Creation of job opportunities
  2. It encourages economic development
  3. Increase in standard of living
  4. It encourages healthy competition among local industries
  5. It leads to specialization
  6. It promotes government revenue.
  7. it encourages effective utilization of natural resources

Free Trade

Free trade is a condition where there is no restriction on goods and services coming in or living a given country.

Protective Measures for Effective International Trade

There are some protective measures taken to protect infant industries in a country during its international trade. 

An infant industry is a newly established industry which cannot comfortably compete with competitors in other industrially more advanced countries. The government take some measures to protect infant industries, for example the use of tariffs.

Tariffs (Import duties): these are taxes imposed by a country on imported goods and services from other countries into the nation.

The Importance of tariffs

  1. It is used to correct balance of payment deficit
  2. Protection of infant industries
  3. Source of revenue for the government.
  4. Prevents the importation of dangerous goods

Trade Barriers

Trade control/ barriers are designed by the government to discourage imports which creates or increases a country's balance of trade surplus and thus increase net exports, but also to protect the domestic economy. These trade barriers are tariffs, imports, and non-tariff barriers. 

Tools Used in controlling international trade

1. Tariffs: these are also known as Import duties; they are taxes imposed by a country on imported goods and services from other countries into the nation.

2. Preferential Duties: these are special duties designed to favour and encourage locally made goods and to discourage the importation of foreign goods and services.

3. Import Quota: this is a quota that specifies the quantity of goods that will come from different countries into a given country.

4. Import License: it is an authority from the government specifying the goods that can be imported by an importer, into the country.

5. Exchange Control: this involves the setting of limits by a country determining the importation power of importers (i.e. the quantity that an importer can import at a given time).

6. Imposition of Embargo: this is straight forward method of controlling international trade which involves banning of importation of goods.

7. Reduction of Excise Duties: by reducing excise duty, locally made goods will be promoted to compete favourably with foreign goods.

8. Devaluation: this involves reducing a countries currency value which will discourage importation into the country and encourage exportation since more money will be realized.

9. Import Monopoly: this is a condition where the government of a country takes over the importation of goods into the country.

Terms in International Trade

The Principle of Comparative Advantage (Cost Advantage)

This was postulated by David Ricardo in the 19th century. The law of comparative costs suggests that a country will produce for export those commodities it can produce more cheaply and import those which it can only produce at higher cost. By this such a country will make much gains from trade.

The principle explains that if countries of the world can specialize in the production of goods and services that they can produce at cheaper rates and leave others that they can produce at higher costs to other countries to produce and then they exchange, this will result in increase in production and the reduction in the cost of production.

Assumptions of the Comparative advantage

  1. There are only two country involved in the exchange of goods and services (bilateral international trade).
  2. Only two commodities are involved in the exchange.
  3. Labour is the only factor of production involved in the production and both countries have equal availability of labour.
  4. Free transportation exists between the two countries
  5. The cost of production is constant
  6. There is always balance of trade between the two countries

 

The balance of trade can be defined as the difference/ relation between value of exports and value of imports over a given period of time. It is the major component of a nation’s balance of payments.

Foreign exchange rate means the rate of exchange between a domestic and a foreign currency, it is the price of one currency in terms of another. The currencies of different nations are traded for each other over foreign exchange markets. The price established for this exchange of currencies is the exchange rate.

Import substitution is an economic theory implemented in order to protect and grow infant domestic industries so that they will emerge to compete with imported goods and make the local economy more self-sufficient and decrease dependence on other nations. This is usually advisable for underdeveloped and developing countries.
