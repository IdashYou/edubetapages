21. INFLATION

Meaning 

Inflation is an economic situation where by there is a persistent rise in the general price level of goods and services in an economy. Price level is determined by finding the price index while the price index is the measure of the average level of price of goods and services. Inflation is a persistent or continuous increase in the general price level. 

Before you can talk of inflation in an economy, prices of most goods and services must have risen but not at the same rate. It is not just when there is rise in price of one or two commodities. Inflation involves a period when the quantity of money in circulation is on the increase and too much money chases few goods and services. 

In other words, the quantity of money in circulation is higher than the quantity of goods and services. This leads to increase in the aggregate demand for goods and services and consequently rise in the general price level. 

Therefore, inflation favours debtors because when they will pay the money they are owing, it will be the same amount but the value is lesser meaning that it can’t buy what it used to buy before; and the creditor loses because the value of his money has fallen. 

Types of Inflation 

1. Demand-Pull inflation

This is a type of inflation that occurs when there is excess demand over supply. It occurs when consumers have high purchasing power. In this case, aggregate demand increases without corresponding increase in the supply of goods and services. 

2. Cost-Push Inflation

This refers to the inflation that occurs as a result of continuous increase in the cost Production.

2. Cost-Push inflation

This is the inflation that occurs as a result of continuous increase in the cost of production. Cost of production includes the payment for labour, capital etc.

3. Creeping Inflation/chronic Inflation

Creeping inflation is a Slow but steady rise in the prices of goods and services. The increase may be about 5% annually. This type of inflation helps in the growth of the economy 

4. Hyper-inflation/galloping inflation

This refers to a rise in the general price level that is persistent and rapid. The prices of goods and services rise quickly causing a dangerous fall in the value of money. This type of inflation causes panic in any economy and it usually occurs after war.

Monetary Policy 

Monetary Policy is a policy set by the Central Bank that involves changing the interest rate and influencing the money supply. It can boost consumer spending through lower interest rates that makes borrowing cheaper on everything from credit cards to mortgage. 

We have two kinds of Monetary policy 

1. Expansionary monetary policy

2. Contractionary monetary policy

Expansionary Monetary Policy

This is a type of monetary policy that aims at increasing the money supply in the economy by decreasing interest rates for loans, purchasing government securities by central banks, and lowering the reserve requirements for banks. It also involves a decrease in discount rate which will reduce the interest paid for getting loans from central bank and encourage spending. An expansionary policy lowers unemployment and stimulates business activities and consumer spending. The overall goal of the expansionary monetary policy is to fuel economic growth. However, if not controlled well it can also possibly lead to higher inflation in the economy.

Contractionary Monetary Policy

This is a type of monetary policy that aims at decreasing the money supply in the economy. It can be achieved by raising interest rates, selling government bonds, and increasing the reserve requirements for banks. It also involves an increase in discount rate which will increase the interest paid for getting loans and thereby discouraging spending. The contractionary policy is utilized when the government wants to control inflation levels.

The Central Bank Can assist in carrying out Government Monetary Policy by

1. Serving as banker to the government: The central bank keeps account for the government. It advises the government with regard to raising loans and helps with financial planning and budget. 

2. It serves as executive instrument for monetary policy. The tools used are: 

  1. Bank rate
  2. Open market operation
  3. Cash ratio/liquidity ratio
  4. Special deposit
  5. Funding
  6. Directives/selective credit control
