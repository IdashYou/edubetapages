9. ELASTICITY

Elasticity is the measurement of the percentage change of one economic variable in response to a change in another.

An elastic variable (with an absolute elasticity value greater than 1) is one which responds more than proportionally to changes in other variables. In contrast, an inelastic variable (with an absolute elasticity value less than 1) is one which changes less than proportionally in response to changes in other variables. 

Elasticity can be quantified as the ratio of the percentage change in one variable to the percentage change in another variable, when the latter variable has a causal influence on the former.

Elasticity in Demand

Elasticity of demand means the rate at which the quantity demanded changes (increase or decrease) in response in response to changes in any of the price of the commodity, income of the consumer of the price of related commodity. 

Perishable goods can neither be increased nor decreased in a short period of time 

Types of Elasticity of Demand

Types of Elasticity of Demand The three major types of elasticity of demand are as follows: 

1. Elastic Demand: Demand is said to be elastic if a change in price or income brings about more than proportional change in the quantity demanded. An example is when 5% increase in price brings about 7% decrease in quantity demanded. The co-efficient of elasticity is greater than one (ED > 1). An increase in price will lead to a fall in total revenue while a decrease in price will increase total revenue. 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 


 

2. Inelastic Demand: Demand is said to be inelastic when a change in price or income brings a less than proportionate change in the quantity 

demanded. Co-efficient of elasticity is less than one (ED<1). An increase in price will increase the total revenue but a decrease in price will lead to a decrease in total revenue. 

40% change in price leads to 10% change in quantity demanded.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 


 

 

3. Unitary or unity elasticity of demand: Demand is said to be unitary if a Change in price or income brings about the same change in quantity demanded. The co-efficient of elasticity is equal to one (ED = 1). A change in price does not bring about any change in total revenue. 

 


 

Degrees of Elasticity of Demand 

i. Perfectly Elastic Demand: Demand is perfectly elastic if a small change in price brings about an infinite change in quantity demanded (that is, a change without limit). The demand curve is parallel to the quantity axis (ED = infinity). 

ii. Fairly elastic demand: Demand is fairly elastic if a small change in price; brings about more than proportionate change in quantity demanded. The co-efficient of elasticity is greater than one but less than infinity (ED > 1 < infinity) the demand curve slopes downward from left to right. 

Elasticity of demand = %Δ in Qty / %Δ in Price

Determinants of Elasticity 

  1. Price of the commodity
  2. Income of the consumer
  3. Price of related commodity
  4. Price Elasticity of Demand

Price of the commodity 

Price elasticity of demand is the degree of responsiveness of the quantity demanded of a commodity to a small change in price. It can also be referred as the ratio of the percentage change in quantity demanded to the percentage change in price. It can be calculated with the following formula

Elasticity of demand = %Δ in Qty / %Δ in Price

Income Elasticity of Demand (IEY)

This can be defined as the degree of responsiveness of quantity demanded oi a commodity to a little change in consumer’s income. In other words, it measures the extent to which consumers change their pattern of demand or consumption as a result of a small change in income. Income elasticity is negative for interior goods since an increase in income will Iead to a decrease in demand. 

Income Elasticity of Demand (IEY) = %Δ in Qty / %Δ in Income

Cross Elasticity of Demand 

It can be defined as the degree of responsiveness of quantity demanded of a commodity to changes in the price of other commodities. Cross elasticity is positive with substitute goods, the co-efficient is greater than zero. It is negative with supplementary goods, its co-efficient is less than zero. 

Cross Elasticity of Demand (IEY) = %Δ in Qty / %Δ in Price of Y

NB: all the formulas are similar is only what you are using to divide that is changing.

Elasticity of Supply 

Elasticity of supply is the degree of responsiveness of the quantity supplied of a commodity to a change in the price of the commodity. 

Types of Elasticity of Supply 

1. Elastic Supply: Supply is elastic when a certain percentage change in price leads to a greater percentage change in the quantity supplied. The co-efficient of elasticity of supply is greater than one (ES>1). 

2. Inelastic Supply: Supply is said .to be inelastic when a percentage change in price brings about a less than proportionate change in quantity supplied of a commodity. The 00efficient of elasticity of supply is less than 1 (ES<1). 

3. Unitary Elasticity of Supply: There is unitary or unity elasticity if a change in price brings about the same proportionate change in the quantity supplied. The co-efficient of elasticity is equal to one (ES = 1).

NB: Inferior goods are goods which its income elasticity of demand is negative. This means that when the income of those consuming it increases, they go for superior products thereby leading to fall in its demand. Example fairly used clothes (okirika).

Normal goods are goods which its income elasticity of demand is positive. This means that a ride in the income of its consumers will lead to increase in its demand. Example new already made clothes.

Perishable goods can neither be increased nor decreased in a short period of time
