11. DIVISION OF LABOUR, SPECIALIZATON AND SCALE OF PRODUCTION

Division of Labour 

Division of Labour is the breaking down of production process into different stages and each stage is undertaken by a person or group of persons. For instance, publishing industry has different stages of operations: typing, plaiting, printing, pinning, trimming and packaging. Each person or group of persons take care of each stage of production and become specialists. 

The idea of division of labour was first demonstrated by Adam Smith in 1776 in his book titled The Wealth of Nations. 

Division of labour brings about exchange. This is because it makes it impossible for one to handle all the aspects of production and therefore lead to the interdependence of people to exchange what they can produce for what they cannot produce. A farmer cannot teach his children but can produce and sell crops, so the money he realizes from selling crops can be used to pay school fess for his children to be taught by a teacher.

Specialization

Specialization is the concentration of individual(s) in a stage of production or a type of job which leads to improvement in skill. In other words, it is the use of resources in their best employment. With Specialization, resources perform duties for which they are best suited. 

Types of Specialization and Division of Labour

1. Specialization by products: here an individual or firm concentrates in the production of a specific commodity. An example is a farmer who concentrates on the production of cocoa.

2. Specialization by Process: here an individual or firm concentrates on a particular stage in the production process. An example is a farmer who concentrates on the harvesting of corns.

3. Territorial Specialization (Geographical Specialization): in this kind of specialization a geographical area or territory concentrates on the production of a particular product which might be as a result of the natural resources available in that area. A typical example is the petroleum industries concentrated in areas that possesses oil as a natural resource. 

4. sex specialization: this is also known as traditional, custom or race specialization. It refers to those kinds of production being carried out mostly by a particular gender. Examples are the train drivers, loaders and truck pushers which are reserved for strong men to do.

Merits/Advantages of Division of Labour and Specialisation

  1. They save time
  2. Saves energy and less fatigue
  3. Encourages technology development
  4. Increases skills of workers
  5. Increases employment opportunity
  6. Reduces cost per unit of output
  7. They encourage the use of machines
  8. They increase the quality of goods produced

Demerits/Disadvantages of Division of Labour and Specialisation

  1. Monotony of work, loss of interest due to repetition
  2. Loss of Craftsmanship and artistry
  3. Increased interdependence
  4. Industrial health hazard for those exposed continuously to toxic substances

Factors that limits Division of Labour

Many things determine the extent of application of division of labour, these factors include

1. The size of the market of the product.

2. The availability of labour.

3. The availability of capital

4. Government Policy

5. Technical challenges and advantage.

6. The nature of the product produced

Scale of Production 

Scale of production refers to the size and capacity of the productive unit. It takes into consideration the quantity of goods produced, the number of workers employed, the number of sales etc. The scale at which a firm Operates is determined by the market and the technical process. There are two types of scale of production: 

Small scale production 

Large scale production 

This division results to two kinds of firms

Small firm and 

Large firm

A firm is a business unit that usually engages in production of goods and services. 

Small Firms engage in small scale production.

Large Firms engage in large scale production.

Internal economies vs Diseconomies 

Internal economies of scale are the advantages a firm get when they produce in large quantities.

Dieconomies of scale are the disadvantages of large-scale production.

Economies of Scale

Economies of scale are the advantages or benefits of large-scale production. It has two components, internal economies and external economies. There are two types of economies of scale

  1. internal economies and internal diseconomies
  2. external economies and external diseconomies

Internal economies of scale

It means the advantages of producing in large quantity which a firm derives within by increasing the size of its output.

These advantages include;

  1. Marketing economies, which are discount for large purchases
  2. Technical economies, which are modern techniques used in massive production.
  3. Financial economies, which is the advantage of easy raising of capital
  4. Welfare economies
  5. Administrative and managerial economies
  6. Research economies
  7. Risk bearing economies

Internal Diseconomies of scale

Note that it is different from external Diseconomies. Internal Diseconomies of scale are the disadvantages of large-scale production. These disadvantages include;

  1. Poor Relationship between the workers and employees
  2. Unfair competition
  3. Slow decision making and slow response to changes
  4. Difficulty in management
  5. Red tapism or bureaucracy

External Economies of Scale

These are the are the advantages enjoyed by large firm when there is concentration of similar firms in a particular area. These advantages include;

  1. Provision of social amenities
  2. Collective research
  3. Healthy competition
  4. Specialisation is possible
  5. Supply of skilled labour
  6. Organized market

External Diseconomies of Scale

These are the disadvantages which a firm experience by staying together/ concentrating with other large and similar firms. These disadvantages include;

  1. Structural unemployment
  2. Environmental pollution
  3. Social problems

Large Firms

Features of Large Firms 

Large firm benefit from both internal and external economies

Employ large number of labourers

Usually engage in Secondary and tertiary production

Requires large capital

Small firms

Features of small firms

  1. Requires small capital
  2. Employed few labourers
  3. Usually engage in primary production

  5. Quick decision making
  6. Easy management

Factors that determine the size of a firm

  1. The Capital
  2. The Market
  3. Entrepreneurial ability
  4. Government policy
  5. Technical nature of the product
  6. Stage of development
  7. The nature of the firm’s product or the nature of the business
  8. The ability of the manager to effectively organize the firm.

5. If a company doubles all its inputs and discovers that its output is more than doubles, we can say that the company is experiencing

A. Increasing Marginal utility

B. Diseconomies of scale

C. Increasing costs

D. Constant returns to scale

E. Increasing returns to scale

Option E. 2019

Explanation

An increasing return to scale occurs when the output increases by a larger proportion than the increase in inputs during the production process.

If the quantity of output rises by a greater proportion—e.g., if output increases by 2.5 times in response to a doubling of all inputs—the production process is said to exhibit increasing returns to scale.
