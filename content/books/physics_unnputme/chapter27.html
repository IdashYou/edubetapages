Waves

Transverse waves are waves which travel perpendicularly (right angles) to the direction of the vibrations producing the waves. E.g. water waves, light waves, and other electromagnetic waves. 

Longitudinal waves are waves which travel in a direction parallel to the vibrations of the medium. E.g. Sound waves and compression wave in coiled Spring. 

Polarization is used in distinguishing transverse and longitudinal waves. The transverse polarization is associated with the direction of the shear stress and displacement in directions perpendicular to the propagation direction, while the longitudinal polarization describes compression of the solid and vibration along the direction of propagation. 

The frequency (f) of wave is the number of complete oscillations per second of the vibrating source producing the wave. It is measured in Hertz. 

Period T is the time for one complete oscillation.

The amplitude of a wave is the maximum displacement of the vibrating particle from its rest position.

De Broglie demonstrated the particle and wave nature of matter. De Broglie's hypothesis is that this relationship λ = , derived for electromagnetic waves, can be adopted to describe matter (e.g. electron, neutron, etc.) as well. A particle of mass traveling with velocity v will have a wavelength λ. The presence of any diffraction effects by matter demonstrated the wave-like nature of matter. 

The properties of waves are reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference, and polarization. Light and other electromagnetic waves can be polarized because they are transverse waves, sound cannot be polarized because it is a longitudinal wave. 

Refraction is due to change of speed when the wave travels from one medium to another. Diffraction is the spreading of waves around obstacles. 

Diffraction is more noticeable when the wavelength is longer than the obstacle. 

Interference effects are produced when two close sources emitting waves of the same frequency and amplitude and wavelength (coherent sources). In constructive interference, the crest of the two waves or their troughs overlap reinforcing each other. In destructive interference, the crest from one wave overlap the trough from the other equal amplitude, and they cancel the effect of each other.

Stationary or standing wave is due to the interference of two waves traveling in opposite direction. E.g. a travelling wave and its reflection.

Conditions for interference

  1. Wave from coherent sources
  2. Same wave amplitude
  3. Same frequency
  4. Wave must be in phase

Terms used in Wave 

1. Amplitude (A): this is the maximum displacement that a wave can undergo during a motion, its unit is meter.

2. Wavelength (ùõå). This is the distance between the successive peak or trough 

3. Cycle. When a wave has undergone maximum displacement up and maximum displacement down and returns to its original position, it has gone through a cycle 

4. Period (T): This s the time it takes a wave to complete one cycle of its motion. It is the time between successive wave crests. its unit is second. 

5. Phase (p): This is the angular distance between a wave that has zero displacement at “t = 0" 

6. Frequency (F): this is the number of cycles a wave complete in one second. Its unit is cycles per second or Hertz. It is the inverse of period, f =1/T.

7. Velocity (V): this is the speed at which a wave crest or peak travels v = wavelength /Period.

 

Reflection is a characteristic of wave that is used for measuring depth of the sea. 

The law of reflection states that the incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal to the surface of the mirror all lie in the same plane. Furthermore, the angle of reflection is equal to the angle of incidence. Both angles are measured with respect to the normal to the mirror.

Rectilinear propagation of light is the phenomenon of light traveling in a straight line. The two natural effects that results from rectilinear phenomenon like shadows and eclipses. Also, the pinhole camera works by the principle.
