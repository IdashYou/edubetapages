Primary cells 

Primary cells are those cells which current is produced as a result of non-reversible chemical changes taking place between the various component of the cell.

Examples of primary cells are Daniel cell and Leclenche Cell

Daniel cell

Its no longer in use. 

Its positive pole is cupper container

Its negative pole is zinc rod

Its electrolyte is Dilute sulphuric Acid

Its depolarizer is copper sulphate solution.

 

Leclenche Cell

We have dry and wet leclenche cell, both are similar but differ in their negative pole electrolyte and their depolarizer.

There positive pole is Carbon rod

The negative pole is Zinc rod in wet, and Zink container in dry

The electrolyte is Ammonium chloride solution in wet and Ammonium chloride in dry.

The depolarizer is Manganese Dioxide in wet but in dry it is Manganese Dioxide + powdered carbon.

Secondary Cells

Secondary Cells or Accumulators are those cells whose chemical actions can be reversed by passing a current opposite in direction of its current. They are rechargeable and last for a long time.

Examples are Lead-acid Accumulator and Nickel-Iron (Alkaline) accumulator.

Accumulator

Its positive pole is Lead peroxide

Its negative pole is Lead

Its electrolyte is Dilute sulphuric Acid

No depolarizer.

CAPACITOR AND RESITOR

Resistance is the opposition that a substance offers to the flow of electric current. ... When an electric current of one ampere passes through a component across which a potential difference (voltage) of one volt exists, then the resistance of that component is one ohm.

Resistance of a wire depends on the length of the wire, the area of the wire and the temperature of the wire.

List of pure metals according to their order of conductivity are silver, copper, gold, aluminium, zinc, nickel, brass, bronze, iron, platinum, steel (carbonized), lead, stainless steel.

A shunt is a specialized resistor of very low resistance that is usually connected in parallel across a galvanometer or a millimeter in order to convert it to an ammeter.

The dielectric ensures that the charges are separated and do not transfer from one plate to the other. The purpose of a capacitor is to store charge, and in a parallel-plate capacitor one plate will take on an excess of positive charge while the other becomes more negative
